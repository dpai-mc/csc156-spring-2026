1. Report Structure Template — Section-by-Section Scaffold

The scaffold below mirrors the Final Project Written Report rubric (150 points). Each collapsible section shows what belongs there, the rubric points at stake, and practical tips. Use this as a checklist while drafting.

A. Cover Page & Case Identification

Rubric connection: Part of Professional Presentation (22 pts total)

Required Elements

  • Report title (e.g., "Digital Forensic Investigation Report — Case #2026-CSC156-[YourInitials]")
  • Case number / reference number
  • Examiner name, title, affiliation (your name, "Student Examiner," Mendocino College)
  • Date of report
  • Classification / distribution marking (e.g., "Academic Exercise — Not for Legal Use")
💡 Tip

Keep the cover page clean — one page maximum. Do not include findings here. This is the first thing an attorney or manager sees; professionalism counts.

B. Table of Contents

Rubric connection: Professional Presentation

  • Auto-generated if using Word/Google Docs heading styles.
  • Include page numbers for all major sections and appendices.
  • Update before final submission (stale TOC = unprofessional impression).
C. Executive Summary (8 pts)

Length: 1–2 pages maximum.

Audience: Non-technical decision-makers (attorney, CISO, HR director).

Content Checklist

  • One-sentence case overview (who requested, what incident, when).
  • Scope of examination (what evidence was provided/examined).
  • Key findings — 3–5 bullet points, plain language, no jargon.
  • Primary conclusions and any recommended actions.
  • Limitations or caveats (e.g., "Mobile device was encrypted and could not be examined").
⚠️ Common Mistake

Do NOT copy-paste your Findings section into the Executive Summary. Summarize at a higher level. If you mention a registry key path here, you have gone too deep.

D. Case Background & Objectives (7 pts)

Describe the context of the investigation:

  • Who requested the examination and why.
  • Brief narrative of the incident or allegation.
  • Specific questions the investigation was asked to answer.
  • Date/time the evidence was received.

Tone: Factual, objective. You are reporting what you were told, not advocating a position.

Explain how you conducted the examination so another examiner could reproduce it.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Listing tools without explaining why you chose them or what you used them for. A tool list alone is not a methodology.

G. Detailed Findings & Analysis (55 pts)

This is the largest scored section. Organize findings by investigative question or by evidence source — pick one scheme and be consistent.

Structure Each Finding

  1. What was found — factual description with artifact path, timestamp, hash.
  2. Where it was found — evidence item #, file path or offset.
  3. How it was found — tool/technique used.
  4. What it means — interpretation in context (clearly labeled as analysis/opinion).
  5. Supporting exhibit reference — "See Exhibit 4, Appendix C."
✅ Good Practice

Use a consistent sub-heading format: Finding [#]: [Brief Description]. Example: "Finding 3: Evidence of Data Exfiltration via USB Device (2026-04-12, 14:32 UTC)."

Separate fact from opinion. Phrases like "The evidence indicates…" or "Based on the recovered artifact, it is my professional opinion that…" signal the transition from fact to analysis.

Provide a chronological narrative or table correlating key events across evidence sources.

  • Use UTC timestamps (note original timezone if relevant).
  • Cite the evidence source for each entry.
  • Highlight corroborated events (multiple independent sources).
  • Mark single-source events explicitly.

I. Conclusions & Recommendations (20 pts)

Summarize your opinions based on the totality of findings. This is where you answer the investigative questions posed in Section D.

  • State each conclusion clearly and link it to the supporting findings.
  • Use qualified language appropriate to the strength of the evidence.
  • Include recommendations for further action (e.g., "Examine the USB device if recovered").
  • Address any questions you were unable to answer and explain why.

See Section 4 below for detailed guidance on handling inconclusive findings.

J. Appendices (15 pts)

Appendices contain supporting material too detailed for the body:

  • Appendix A: Chain of Custody forms
  • Appendix B: Hash verification logs (full SHA-256 values)
  • Appendix C: Exhibits (screenshots, annotated images, tool output)
  • Appendix D: Complete timeline export (if too long for body)
  • Appendix E: Glossary of technical terms
💡 Tip

Number exhibits sequentially (Exhibit 1, Exhibit 2…) and reference them in the body. Never include an exhibit that is not referenced in the main text.

2. Writing for Mixed Audiences

A single investigation often produces deliverables for three distinct audiences. Your capstone report is primarily a technical report, but it must include an Executive Summary suitable for non-technical readers and exhibits suitable for presentation. Understanding all three audiences makes you a more effective communicator.

👔 Audience 1: Executive / Attorney

Who They Are

C-suite executives, HR directors, general counsel, prosecutors, or civil litigators. They make decisions based on your findings but rarely have technical backgrounds.

What They Need

  • Bottom-line conclusions — "Did the employee steal data? Yes/No/Inconclusive."
  • Business impact — what was compromised, what is the risk.
  • Confidence level — how certain are you?
  • Recommended next steps in plain language.

Language Guidance

  • Avoid: "The $UsnJrnl entry at offset 0x4A72F shows a FILE_CREATE operation for client_db.xlsx with parent MFT reference 0x1A3C."
  • Use instead: "Windows system logs confirm that the file 'client_db.xlsx' was copied to a removable USB drive on April 12, 2026 at 2:32 PM."

Format Tips

  • Bullet points over paragraphs.
  • Visual timeline graphics rather than raw log tables.
  • Define any technical term you must use.
🖥️ Audience 2: Technical Manager / IT Director

Who They Are

IT directors, security operations managers, incident response leads. They understand technology but may not be forensic specialists.

What They Need

  • Enough technical detail to understand the attack vector and remediate.
  • Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) they can feed into security tools.
  • Scope of compromise — which systems, which accounts, which data.
  • Remediation recommendations with technical specificity.

Language Guidance

  • Appropriate: "The attacker used PsExec to move laterally from WORKSTATION-04 to SERVER-DC01 using compromised domain admin credentials (jsmith_admin)."
  • Too deep: Full hex dumps or raw Volatility output in the body (put these in appendices).

Format Tips

  • IOC tables (IP addresses, file hashes, registry keys).
  • Network diagrams showing lateral movement.
  • Reference appendix exhibits for raw evidence.
🔬 Audience 3: Peer Examiner / Opposing Expert

Who They Are

Another forensic examiner reviewing your work for quality assurance, or an opposing expert retained to challenge your findings in litigation.

What They Need

  • Complete methodology — every step, every tool, every version number.
  • Reproducibility — could they reach the same findings independently?
  • Hash verification at every stage.
  • Raw evidence references (file paths, offsets, timestamps with timezone).
  • Acknowledgment of limitations and alternative interpretations.

Language Guidance

  • Full technical precision is expected and appropriate.
  • Cite tool output with exhibit numbers.
  • Distinguish clearly between automated tool results and your manual analysis.

Format Tips

  • Detailed methodology section with step-by-step procedures.
  • Complete hash logs in appendices.
  • Version-controlled tool output as exhibits.
✅ Capstone Application

Your capstone report should satisfy all three audiences: the Executive Summary serves Audience 1, the body serves Audiences 2 and 3, and the Appendices provide the raw evidence trail for Audience 3. The rubric category "Professional Presentation" (22 pts) specifically evaluates whether you adapt your language appropriately across sections.

3. Exhibit Preparation Standards

Exhibits are the evidence that supports your written findings. Poorly prepared exhibits undermine credibility; well-prepared exhibits make your report persuasive and defensible.

General Exhibit Rules

  • Number sequentially: Exhibit 1, Exhibit 2, etc.
  • Every exhibit referenced in the body must appear in the Appendix (and vice versa).
  • Include a caption: exhibit number, brief description, source evidence item, date/time if applicable.
  • Redact sensitive information (PII, passwords) unless relevant to the finding.
📸 Screenshots

Do

  • Capture the full tool window including the title bar (shows tool name/version).
  • Add annotation callouts (arrows, boxes) highlighting the relevant artifact.
  • Include a visible timestamp or clock reference where possible.
  • Use high resolution — text must be readable when printed.

Don't

  • Crop so aggressively that context is lost.
  • Use phone photos of a monitor (screenshot tools exist for a reason).
  • Leave personal bookmarks, unrelated tabs, or desktop clutter visible.
  • Resize images so small that text becomes illegible.
🔐 Hash Verification Logs

Do

  • Include both the acquisition hash and the verification hash side by side.
  • State the algorithm (SHA-256 preferred; MD5 alone is insufficient).
  • Show the tool that computed the hash and its output.
  • Record the date/time of verification.

Don't

  • Truncate hash values (show the full string).
  • Omit the verification step — a single hash without re-verification proves nothing about integrity.
  • Mix up source hash vs. copy hash in your table.
📅 Timeline Excerpts

Do

  • Use a consistent timestamp format (ISO 8601: YYYY-MM-DD HH:MM:SS UTC).
  • Cite the source for each timeline entry.
  • Highlight the critical events relevant to your finding.
  • Note timezone conversions explicitly if original source uses local time.

Don't

  • Dump a 500-row Plaso export into the body — put it in an appendix and excerpt key rows.
  • Mix timezones without noting which is which.
  • Include timeline entries unrelated to your findings (noise reduces clarity).
🛠️ Tool Output (Volatility, RegRipper, Autopsy, etc.)

Do

  • Show the exact command or query used to produce the output.
  • Highlight or annotate the relevant lines.
  • State the tool version and the evidence file it was run against.
  • Use monospace formatting for terminal/console output.

Don't

  • Include pages of unfiltered output with no annotation.
  • Omit the command — reviewers need to know how to reproduce.
  • Screenshot tool output at low resolution where text is unreadable.
⚠️ Exhibit Integrity Rule

Never alter evidence screenshots to change what they show. Annotations (arrows, boxes, highlights) are acceptable; editing content is fabrication. If you must crop, ensure nothing misleading results from the crop.

4. Handling Inconclusive or Ambiguous Findings

Not every investigation question has a clear answer. Professional examiners must communicate uncertainty honestly without undermining the report's overall credibility. This is one of the hardest professional skills to develop — and the rubric's "Analysis Quality" (30 pts) evaluates it directly.

When Findings Are Inconclusive

  • The evidence supports multiple interpretations and you cannot eliminate alternatives.
  • Critical evidence is missing, encrypted, or corrupted.
  • A single artifact supports a conclusion, but no corroborating evidence exists.
  • Timestamps are unreliable (clock skew, anti-forensics, timezone ambiguity).

Language Patterns for Inconclusive Conclusions

Strength LevelLanguage PatternWhen to Use
Strong conclusion"The evidence demonstrates that…" / "The examination confirmed…"Multiple corroborating sources; no viable alternative explanation
Probable conclusion"The evidence is consistent with…" / "The findings suggest…"Strong evidence but one or more alternative explanations remain possible
Possible conclusion"The evidence does not exclude the possibility that…"Limited evidence; multiple explanations equally viable
Inconclusive"The examination was unable to determine…" / "Insufficient evidence exists to conclude…"Evidence is absent, contradictory, or unreliable

Professional Standards for Uncertainty

  1. State what you DID find — even if inconclusive, document the artifacts examined.
  2. Explain WHY it is inconclusive — missing evidence, encryption, anti-forensics, scope limitation.
  3. Identify what COULD resolve it — "Examination of the suspect's mobile device may provide corroborating evidence."
  4. Never speculate beyond the evidence — if you cannot support it, do not write it.
  5. Do not apologize — inconclusive findings are a normal, professional outcome. Present them matter-of-factly.
🔑 Key Principle

An honest "inconclusive" is always better than an overreaching conclusion. Overstating findings destroys examiner credibility in court and violates professional ethics (Week 3, IACIS Code of Ethics). The rubric rewards appropriate qualification of conclusions.

Single-Source Findings

A finding supported by only one artifact (no corroboration) requires special treatment:

  • Explicitly note it is single-source: "This finding is based solely on [artifact]. No independent corroboration was identified."
  • Explain what corroboration you looked for and did not find.
  • Assess the reliability of the single source (e.g., "Windows Event Logs are considered a reliable system-generated record").
  • Use "probable" or "possible" language rather than definitive conclusions.

5. Discussion 16: Peer Methodology Review

Graded — 3 Points CSLO 4

📌 This Is Your Only Graded Assignment Due This Week

Discussion 16 is worth 3 points and is due by the end of Week 16. It is the sole graded deliverable this week (the capstone project is due Week 17).

Purpose

Professional forensic practice includes peer review — another qualified examiner reviews your methodology to identify gaps, verify reproducibility, and strengthen your report before submission. This discussion simulates that process.

Instructions

Initial Post (Due: Day 3 — Wednesday 11:59 PM)

Write a post (minimum 150 words) that describes the methodology you used in your capstone investigation. Include:

  1. Evidence types examined — what categories of evidence did you analyze? (e.g., disk image, memory dump, network capture, mobile extraction)
  2. Tools and techniques — which tools did you use for each evidence type and why?
  3. Analysis sequence — in what order did you examine the evidence and why?
  4. Challenges encountered — what difficulties arose and how did you address them?
  5. One area of uncertainty — identify one aspect of your methodology you are unsure about or would like peer input on.
⚠️ Academic Integrity — Critical

Share your METHODOLOGY only — not your findings or conclusions. Describing how you investigated is appropriate; revealing what you found compromises the independence of other students' work. Posts that disclose specific findings, artifact content, or conclusions will receive zero credit and may be referred for academic integrity review.

Peer Response (Due: Day 5 — Friday 11:59 PM)

Respond to at least one classmate's post (minimum 75 words) with a substantive methodology critique. Your response should include at least one of the following:

  • A gap you notice — an evidence type or technique they might have missed.
  • An alternative approach — a different tool or sequence that might yield additional results.
  • A validation question — asking how they verified a particular step.
  • A shared challenge — describing how you solved a similar problem differently.

Generic responses ("Good job!" / "I agree") do not earn credit.

Question 1: Rewriting for Audience

A forensic examiner writes the following in their report body:

"Analysis of the $UsnJrnl at MFT offset 0x4A72F revealed a FILE_CREATE operation (reason code 0x100) for 'client_db.xlsx' with a parent directory reference of MFT entry 0x1A3C, corresponding to the E:\ volume root, at timestamp 2026-04-12T14:32:07Z."

Rewrite this finding in 1–2 sentences suitable for the Executive Summary (non-technical attorney audience). Then click below to compare your version.

Example rewrite: "Windows system records confirm that the file 'client_db.xlsx' was created on a removable USB drive (E:\ drive) on April 12, 2026 at 2:32 PM UTC, indicating the file was copied to an external device."

Why this works: It removes jargon ($UsnJrnl, MFT offset, reason codes), uses plain language ("Windows system records"), preserves the essential fact (file copied to USB, date/time), and states the significance ("indicating the file was copied to an external device"). The technical detail belongs in the Findings section and appendix exhibits — not the Executive Summary.

Question 2: Single-Source Finding in Conclusions

Your investigation found a single Windows Event Log entry (Event ID 4624, Logon Type 10) showing a remote desktop connection from IP 192.168.1.105 to the server at 03:14 UTC. No other evidence (firewall logs, network capture, or endpoint detection) corroborates this connection. The network logs for that time window are missing due to a log rotation policy.

Which of the following is the most appropriate way to present this in your Conclusions section?

  1. "The server was accessed remotely from 192.168.1.105 at 03:14 UTC."
  2. "A Windows Security Event Log entry records a remote desktop logon from 192.168.1.105 at 03:14 UTC. This finding is based on a single source; network logs that could corroborate the connection were unavailable due to log rotation. The Event Log is a system-generated record considered reliable, though independent verification was not possible."
  3. "It is impossible to determine whether remote access occurred because the network logs are missing."
  4. "The remote access probably happened but cannot be proven."

Best answer: B

Explanation:

  • A is too definitive — it presents a single-source finding as established fact without noting the limitation.
  • B correctly states what was found, identifies it as single-source, explains why corroboration is unavailable, and assesses the reliability of the source. This is the professional standard.
  • C understates the evidence — you DID find something; dismissing it entirely is inaccurate.
  • D is informal and vague — "probably" and "proven" are not appropriate forensic report language.

Question 3: Peer Methodology Feedback

A classmate posts the following methodology description in Discussion 16:

"I used Autopsy to analyze the disk image and found several deleted files. I also looked at the registry for USB device history. I used Wireshark to look at the network capture. My analysis took about 4 hours total."

Identify at least two gaps in this methodology description that you would raise in a peer response. Click below to compare.

Possible gaps to raise (any two or more is appropriate):

  • No tool versions listed — which version of Autopsy? Wireshark? This matters for reproducibility and Daubert compliance.
  • No analysis sequence/rationale — why that order? Did they start with volatile data? Was there a reason to prioritize disk over network?
  • No hash verification mentioned — did they verify the image integrity before analysis?
  • No keyword search strategy described — how did they decide what to look for?
  • Registry analysis tool not specified — Autopsy's registry viewer? RegRipper? Manual review?
  • "Found several deleted files" is a finding, not methodology — the post should describe the technique (file carving, recycle bin analysis) not the result.
  • No mention of challenges or uncertainty — required element of the Discussion 16 prompt.

A strong peer response would identify 1–2 of these gaps and suggest specific improvements (e.g., "You might consider documenting which Volatility plugins you used and in what order, since that affects reproducibility").
