1. Investigation Lifecycle Review — The 7-Phase Model

Throughout this course you have learned a structured, repeatable investigation methodology. The seven phases below are your roadmap from incident notification to case closure. In Week 16, you are primarily operating in Phases 2–4, with planning hooks into Phases 5–7.

Identification & Notification — Recognize that an incident has occurred; receive authorization to investigate.
Week 16 focus: Your capstone scenario provides this. Confirm you understand the incident type and scope.
Planning & Preparation — Define objectives, scope, legal authority, team roles, and resource requirements.
Week 16 focus: Primary activity this module. You will produce a written investigation plan.
Evidence Acquisition — Create forensically sound images/copies; document chain of custody.
Week 16 focus: Covered in Module 16.2. Your plan must specify what to acquire and how.
Examination & Analysis — Apply tools and techniques to extract and interpret artifacts.
Week 16 focus: Covered in Modules 16.3–16.4. Your plan identifies which analysis techniques apply.
Documentation & Reporting — Record findings in a structured forensic report; maintain contemporaneous notes.
Week 16 focus: Begins now (your plan is a living document) and culminates in Week 17.
Presentation — Communicate findings to stakeholders (legal, management, jury) clearly and defensibly.
Week 16 focus: Plan your report audience and format now; deliver in Week 17.
Case Closure & Lessons Learned — Archive evidence, document lessons learned, recommend preventive controls.
Week 16 focus: Note recommendations as you analyze; finalize in Week 17.
Deeper Dive: How the Phases Map to Professional Standards

The 7-phase model synthesizes guidance from multiple professional frameworks:

  • NIST SP 800-86 — Collection → Examination → Analysis → Reporting
  • SWGDE/ISO 27037 — Identification → Collection → Acquisition → Preservation
  • ACPO Good Practice Guide — Four principles of digital evidence handling

Your capstone report should reference at least one of these frameworks to demonstrate professional alignment.

💡 Tip

The phases are iterative, not strictly linear. During analysis (Phase 4) you may discover new evidence sources, sending you back to Phase 3. Your plan should anticipate this by including a "scope change" procedure.

3. Creating Your Investigation Plan

The investigation plan is your operational blueprint. It ensures your work is systematic, defensible, and repeatable. Use the template below as a starting framework — adapt it to your specific scenario.

Plan Components

  1. Case Header — Case ID, examiner name, date initiated, authorization reference.
  2. Incident Summary — What happened? When was it reported? Who is involved?
  3. Investigation Objectives — What questions must you answer? (Be specific.)
  4. Scope & Boundaries — What systems/data are in scope? What is explicitly out of scope?
  5. Evidence Sources — List each evidence item with domain, location, and acquisition method.
  6. Tool Selection & Justification — Which tools will you use and why?
  7. Analysis Approach — Ordered steps for each evidence source.
  8. Timeline — Target milestones mapped to Week 16 modules and Week 17 deliverable.
  9. Legal/Ethical Considerations — Authority, privacy boundaries, escalation triggers.
  10. Risk & Contingency — What could go wrong? What is your fallback?

💡 Tip: Objectives Drive Everything

Write your investigation objectives as specific, answerable questions. Compare:

  • ❌ Weak: "Find out what happened."
  • ✅ Strong: "Determine whether User X copied files from the R&D share to a USB device between March 1–15, 2026."

Specific objectives keep your analysis focused and make your report conclusions directly traceable.

4. Chain of Custody & Evidence Integrity

No matter how brilliant your analysis, findings are worthless if you cannot demonstrate that evidence was handled properly. Chain of custody (CoC) documentation proves that evidence has not been altered, tampered with, or mishandled from acquisition through presentation.

Core Principles (Review)

  • Integrity: Hash values (MD5 + SHA-256 minimum) computed at acquisition and verified at every transfer.
  • Continuity: Every person who handles evidence is logged with date, time, and purpose.
  • Accountability: Evidence is stored securely; access is controlled and logged.
  • Documentation: Contemporaneous notes — record actions as they happen, not from memory later.

⚠️ Common Capstone Mistake

Students often create a single CoC entry at the start and never update it. Your capstone report should include at minimum:

  • An entry when evidence is first acquired (image created)
  • An entry when you begin analysis on the image
  • A verification entry confirming hash match before drawing conclusions

Hash Verification — Why Both MD5 and SHA-256?

MD5 is fast and widely supported by legacy tools, but it is cryptographically broken (collisions are feasible). SHA-256 provides collision resistance suitable for legal proceedings. Using both provides backward compatibility and cryptographic assurance.

Deeper Dive: What Breaks Chain of Custody?

In professional practice and courtroom settings, the following can invalidate evidence:

  • Gaps in the log (unaccounted time periods)
  • Missing or mismatched hash values
  • Analysis performed on original media instead of a forensic copy
  • Unauthorized personnel accessing evidence
  • Improper storage (unlocked room, unencrypted digital storage)

For your capstone: treat your forensic images as if they would be presented in court. Document accordingly.

5. Ethical Decision-Making in Digital Forensics

Digital forensic examiners regularly encounter data that is sensitive, unexpected, or outside the investigation scope. You need a consistent decision framework to handle these situations professionally.

The Document → Do Not Analyze → Escalate → Note Framework

ℹ️ Framework Steps

  1. Document — Record that you encountered the material (type, location, timestamp). Do not describe content in detail.
  2. Do Not Analyze — Stop examining that specific data. Do not open, copy, or extract it beyond what was necessary to identify it.
  3. Escalate — Immediately notify the appropriate authority (supervisor, legal counsel, law enforcement if required).
  4. Note — Add a note to your case file that you followed this procedure, including who you notified and when.

Practice Scenarios

For each scenario below, consider how you would apply the framework. Expand to see the recommended response.

Scenario A: Personal Medical Records

While examining a suspect's workstation for evidence of intellectual property theft, you discover a folder containing the suspect's personal medical records (doctor's notes, prescriptions, lab results). These are clearly unrelated to your investigation scope.

Recommended Response — Scenario A
  • Document: Note the folder path, that it contains personal medical information, and the timestamp of discovery.
  • Do Not Analyze: Do not read, copy, or include any medical content in your report.
  • Escalate: Not typically required unless your authorization is ambiguous — confirm with case lead that personal medical data is out of scope.
  • Note: Record in your case notes that the folder was identified and excluded from analysis per scope boundaries and privacy considerations.

Scenario B: Possible Child Exploitation Material

During a network intrusion investigation, you encounter image thumbnails in an unallocated space recovery that appear to depict child sexual abuse material (CSAM).

Recommended Response — Scenario B
  • Document: Record the exact location (sector/offset), timestamp, and that the material appears to be CSAM. Do NOT describe images in detail.
  • Do Not Analyze: Immediately stop examining that data. Do not open full-size images, do not copy them to separate media.
  • Escalate: Mandatory and immediate. Notify your supervisor and legal counsel. In the United States, this triggers mandatory reporting to law enforcement (and potentially NCMEC). You may have legal obligations as a mandatory reporter.
  • Note: Document the exact time you stopped, who you contacted, and preserve the evidence in its current state for law enforcement.

Key point: This is the highest-priority escalation scenario. Your legal and ethical obligation overrides the original investigation scope.

Scenario C: Evidence of a Different Crime

You are investigating unauthorized access to a database. While reviewing browser history, you find evidence suggesting the suspect is conducting an illegal gambling operation from their work computer.

Recommended Response — Scenario C
  • Document: Record the artifact location, type (browser history entries), and general nature (apparent illegal gambling activity).
  • Do Not Analyze: Do not pivot your investigation to the gambling activity. It is outside your authorized scope.
  • Escalate: Notify your supervisor and/or legal counsel. They will determine whether to expand the investigation scope (requiring new authorization) or refer to a separate investigation.
  • Note: Record that you identified potentially relevant information outside scope and escalated appropriately. Continue your original authorized investigation.

Key point: "Plain view" doctrine may apply in law enforcement contexts, but as a forensic examiner you should never unilaterally expand scope. Always get authorization.

💡 Capstone Application

In your capstone report, include a brief "Ethical Considerations" section. Even if your scenario does not trigger the framework, state what you would do if you encountered out-of-scope sensitive material. This demonstrates professional awareness.
