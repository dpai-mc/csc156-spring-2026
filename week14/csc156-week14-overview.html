Week 14: Forensic Reporting & Expert Testimony

From Technical Analysis to Professional Communication

📅 Week 14 of 17 📚 Lecture: 2 hrs 🔬 Lab: 3 hrs ⏰ Est. Total: ~9 hrs

👋Welcome to Week 14

This week marks a pivotal shift in the course. Over the past thirteen weeks you have built a deep technical toolkit — imaging drives, analyzing registries and memory, dissecting packets, reverse-engineering malware, and more. None of that work matters in a real investigation if you cannot communicate your findings clearly, accurately, and defensibly.

Week 14 bridges the gap between doing forensics and presenting forensics. You will learn the documentation standards that make or break a case, study the anatomy of a professional forensic report, explore the legal standards governing expert testimony, and practice the quality-assurance process that ensures your report can withstand scrutiny in any setting — from a corporate boardroom to a courtroom witness stand.

🔗 Course Arc Connection Every technical skill you have developed since Week 1 feeds directly into this week's deliverables. Think of Week 14 as the place where your investigation skills become evidence — documented, structured, and ready for the people who need them most.

🎯Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 14, you will be able to:

  1. Explain why thorough documentation is the foundation of every defensible forensic investigation and identify the professional standards that govern forensic reporting.
  2. Describe the complete anatomy of a professional forensic report, including all major sections from the executive summary through appendices.
  3. Distinguish between statements of fact and statements of opinion in forensic writing and apply the rules for expressing each appropriately.
  4. Explain the legal standards for expert witness qualification and testimony, including the Daubert standard, the Frye standard, and Federal Rule of Evidence 702.
  5. Describe effective courtroom testimony techniques, including direct examination, cross-examination strategies, and common impeachment tactics.
  6. Apply a three-level quality-assurance review process (self-review, technical peer review, administrative/legal review) to finalize a forensic report for submission.
📊 CSLO Alignment This week directly supports CSLO 4: Be able to generate reports which detail the approach and provide an audit trail that supports the integrity of the investigation process and any actions taken.

🧠Key Concepts & Themes

This week covers a rich set of professional-practice topics. Expand the panel below for a quick-reference list.

View Key Concepts for Week 14
  • Documentation standards — SWGDE, NIST SP 800-86, ISO 27037 guidelines for forensic documentation
  • The "hit by a bus" test — Could another qualified examiner reproduce your work from your notes alone?
  • Report anatomy (10 sections) — Executive summary, authorization, evidence inventory, tools & methodology, findings, analysis, conclusions, opinions, appendices, and chain-of-custody references
  • Fact vs. opinion — Distinguishing objective observations from expert interpretations; rules for expressing each in a forensic report
  • Daubert standard — Federal test for admissibility of expert testimony (testing, peer review, error rate, general acceptance)
  • Frye standard — "General acceptance" test still used in some state jurisdictions
  • Federal Rule of Evidence 702 — Statutory framework for qualifying expert witnesses and admitting expert opinion
  • Expert witness qualification — Education, training, experience, certification, and prior testimony history
  • Cross-examination strategies — Impeachment tactics, hypothetical challenges, and how to maintain credibility on the stand
  • Three-level QA process — Self-review (examiner), technical peer review (second examiner), and administrative/legal review (supervisor or counsel)
  • Final Practical connection — How this week's reporting skills feed directly into the Final Practical Project report due in Weeks 16–17
