üìö Why Study Case Studies?

Case studies transform abstract legal principles into concrete, memorable examples.

Throughout Modules 1-4, you've learned about Federal Rules of Evidence, ethics codes, privacy laws, and expert testimony. But how do these principles actually apply in real investigations? Case studies show you:

  • How legal principles work (or fail) in practice
  • What happens when investigators make mistakes
  • How courts interpret laws in specific situations
  • What forensic techniques were successful (and why)
  • The real-world consequences of legal and ethical decisions

üìñ What is a Legal Precedent?

A legal precedent (also called "case law") is a court decision that establishes a principle or rule that courts must follow when deciding subsequent cases with similar issues or facts. Under the doctrine of stare decisis ("to stand by things decided"), lower courts are bound by decisions of higher courts in their jurisdiction.

Why This Matters for Digital Forensics: Landmark cases like Riley v. California and Carpenter v. United States establish legal rules that govern how you can conduct digital investigations. Understanding these precedents helps you stay within legal boundaries and anticipate how courts will rule on evidence admissibility.

üîç Case Study 1: BTK Killer (Dennis Rader)

Serial Murder Investigation - 2005

The Case That Metadata Solved

üìã Background

Dennis Rader terrorized Wichita, Kansas, for over 30 years, killing 10 people between 1974 and 1991. He called himself "BTK" (Bind, Torture, Kill) and taunted police with letters describing his crimes. After going silent for years, Rader resumed communication with police in 2004.

In one communication, Rader asked police: "Can I communicate with floppy [disk] and not be traced to a computer?" Police assured him it was safe (which was false). Trusting them, Rader sent a purple floppy disk to a local TV station containing a document.

üî¨ Forensic Breakthrough

The Evidence: Purple floppy disk containing a Microsoft Word document

Forensic Technique: Document metadata extraction

What Investigators Found:

  • Author Name: "Dennis"
  • Last Modified By: "Christ Lutheran Church"
  • Document Path: Revealed previous file locations
  • Deleted Content: Recovered from unallocated space on floppy

The Connection: A simple Google search for "Christ Lutheran Church Wichita Dennis" led directly to Dennis Rader, who was president of the church council. DNA evidence from his daughter confirmed the match.

üìñ Forensic Lessons Learned

  1. Metadata Persistence: Document metadata persists even when files are deleted. Always examine document properties, not just content.
  2. Hidden Information: Digital evidence contains layers of information invisible to casual users. Criminals often underestimate what forensic examiners can recover.
  3. Multiple Evidence Types: Metadata provided the lead, but DNA confirmed identity. Digital and physical evidence work together.
  4. Simple Techniques Work: This wasn't sophisticated hacking or advanced malware analysis‚Äîjust basic metadata examination combined with Google search.
  5. Social Engineering: Police used social engineering (lying to Rader about traceability) to elicit digital evidence. While controversial, courts upheld this tactic.

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Principles Applied

  • FRE Rule 901 (Authentication): Metadata authenticated the document's origin
  • FRE Rule 401 (Relevance): Metadata was directly relevant to identifying BTK
  • Chain of Custody: Disk was properly documented from receipt to analysis
  • No Fourth Amendment Issue: Rader voluntarily sent the disk to police

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Outcome

Arrest: February 25, 2005

Charges: 10 counts of first-degree murder

Plea: Guilty (to avoid death penalty)

Sentence: 10 consecutive life terms (minimum 175 years) without possibility of parole

Evidence Admissibility: All digital evidence admitted without challenge. Rader's voluntary submission of the disk eliminated Fourth Amendment concerns.

üéØ Practical Takeaway

Always examine metadata thoroughly. Document properties, EXIF data, and file system metadata often contain identifying information that suspects don't realize exists. This simple technique has solved countless cases.

Tools for Metadata Extraction: ExifTool, Metadata Assistant, built-in forensic tool metadata viewers

üìß Case Study 2: Enron Corporation Scandal

Corporate Fraud Investigation - 2001-2006

The Largest Email Forensics Investigation in History

üìã Background

Enron Corporation was once America's seventh-largest company, valued at $70 billion. In 2001, the energy giant collapsed spectacularly when massive accounting fraud was exposed. Executives had systematically hidden billions of dollars in debt through complex financial schemes and special purpose entities while inflating profits.

The investigation required proving that executives knew about the fraud and intentionally misled investors, auditors, and employees. This required examining millions of internal communications to establish knowledge, intent, and conspiracy.

üî¨ Forensic Breakthrough

The Evidence: Over 500,000 emails from Enron employees (largest email corpus ever analyzed at the time)

Forensic Technique: Large-scale email forensics and communication pattern analysis

What Investigators Found:

  • Executive Knowledge: Emails showing top executives discussing "accounting irregularities" and schemes to "hide debt"
  • Insider Trading: Communications revealing executives sold stock while knowing company was failing
  • Conspiracy Evidence: Email chains showing coordination among executives to mislead auditors
  • Timeline of Fraud: Email dates established when executives knew about problems
  • Attempts to Destroy Evidence: Mass email deletions after investigation began

Analysis Methods:

  • Keyword searches: "accounting irregularities," "hide," "off-books," "Fastow" (CFO name)
  • Communication network analysis: Who communicated with whom about fraud
  • Timeline analysis: When did executives learn about problems?
  • Pattern analysis: Identifying coordinated responses to auditor questions

üìñ Forensic Lessons Learned

  1. Email is Permanent: Even "deleted" emails can be recovered from backups, servers, and recipient mailboxes. Employees believe email is ephemeral; forensic examiners know it's permanent.
  2. Scale Matters: Large-scale email analysis requires specialized tools and techniques. Manual review of 500,000+ emails is impossible.
  3. Context is Critical: Individual emails may seem innocent, but patterns across multiple communications reveal intent and knowledge.
  4. Communication Networks: Analyzing who communicated with whom reveals organizational structure and conspiracy.
  5. Keyword Selection: Effective keywords require understanding the business and fraud schemes. Generic searches miss critical evidence.
  6. Preservation is Essential: Email must be preserved immediately when investigation begins. Litigation holds prevent deletion.

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Principles Applied

  • FRE Rule 803(6) (Business Records): Emails admitted as business records under hearsay exception
  • FRE Rule 901 (Authentication): Email headers, server logs, and metadata authenticated messages
  • FRE Rule 401 (Relevance): Emails directly relevant to proving fraud and conspiracy
  • Criminal Intent: Emails established "mens rea" (guilty mind) necessary for criminal conviction
  • Spoliation: Attempts to delete emails after investigation began showed consciousness of guilt

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Outcomes

CEO Jeffrey Skilling: Convicted on 19 counts; sentenced to 24 years (later reduced to 14 years)

Chairman Kenneth Lay: Convicted on 10 counts; died before sentencing (conviction vacated)

CFO Andrew Fastow: Pleaded guilty; cooperated with prosecutors; sentenced to 6 years

Arthur Andersen (Accounting Firm): Convicted of obstruction (later overturned by Supreme Court, but firm effectively destroyed)

Total Convictions: Over 30 Enron executives and employees convicted

Evidence Admissibility: Email evidence was central to convictions. Courts accepted forensic authentication methods and admitted emails under business records exception.

üéØ Practical Takeaways

  1. Preserve Email Early: Issue litigation hold immediately when investigation begins
  2. Search Strategically: Develop keyword lists based on understanding of the alleged conduct
  3. Analyze Patterns: Look beyond individual emails to communication networks and patterns
  4. Timeline Everything: Email timestamps establish what executives knew and when
  5. Expect Spoliation: Sophisticated actors will attempt to destroy evidence; plan accordingly

üéØ Case Study 3: Target Data Breach

Network Intrusion Investigation - 2013

The Breach That Changed Retail Security

üìã Background

In November-December 2013, hackers compromised Target Corporation's payment systems during the critical holiday shopping season. The breach resulted in:

  • 40 million credit and debit card numbers stolen
  • 70 million customer records compromised (names, addresses, phones, emails)
  • $202 million in direct costs to Target
  • $18.5 million settlement with 47 states
  • Hundreds of millions in bank reimbursements
  • CEO and CIO resignations
  • Massive reputational damage

üî¨ Forensic Investigation

The Challenge: How did attackers breach Target's network, install malware on thousands of point-of-sale (POS) systems, and exfiltrate massive amounts of data?

Attack Timeline Reconstructed Through Forensics:

Step 1: Initial Compromise (Mid-November 2013)

Entry Point: Hackers stole credentials from Fazio Mechanical Services, a small HVAC contractor that had remote access to Target's network for monitoring energy consumption.

Forensic Evidence: Network logs showed login from Fazio's credentials to Target's vendor portal.

Step 2: Lateral Movement (Late November)

Network Penetration: Attackers moved from vendor portal to Target's internal network, exploiting weak network segmentation.

Forensic Evidence: Network traffic analysis revealed unauthorized connections from vendor network segment to internal corporate systems.

Step 3: Malware Deployment (November 27)

POS Infection: Custom RAM-scraping malware ("POSWDS") installed on 1,797 point-of-sale registers across Target stores.

Forensic Evidence: Memory forensics revealed malware in RAM of POS systems. Reverse engineering showed malware scraped credit card data from memory before encryption.

Step 4: Data Exfiltration (November 30 - December 17)

Data Theft: Malware sent stolen card data to internal Target servers, then attackers exfiltrated to external servers in Russia and Brazil.

Forensic Evidence: Network forensics tracked data flows from POS systems ‚Üí internal staging servers ‚Üí external command-and-control servers.

Step 5: Failure to Respond (Throughout)

Ignored Alerts: Target's FireEye security system detected and alerted on malware, but security team failed to investigate or respond.

Forensic Evidence: FireEye logs showed multiple alerts that were not acted upon. This became critical evidence in determining liability.

Forensic Techniques Used:

  • Network Forensics: Analysis of firewall logs, NetFlow data, IDS/IPS alerts
  • Memory Forensics: RAM dumps from POS systems to identify malware
  • Malware Analysis: Reverse engineering of POSWDS to understand functionality
  • Timeline Analysis: Correlating events across multiple systems and log sources
  • Indicator of Compromise (IOC) Development: Identifying signatures to find all infected systems

üìñ Forensic Lessons Learned

  1. Third-Party Risk: Vendors with network access are potential attack vectors. Forensic investigations must examine third-party access logs.
  2. Network Segmentation Matters: Weak segmentation allowed attackers to move from vendor network to POS systems. Forensics revealed the exploitation path.
  3. Memory Forensics is Critical: RAM-scraping malware doesn't persist on disk. Memory dumps were essential to identifying malware.
  4. Alert Fatigue is Real: Security tools generated alerts, but humans failed to respond. Forensics documented this failure, establishing liability.
  5. Timeline Reconstruction: Correlating logs from multiple sources (firewalls, servers, POS systems, security tools) reconstructed complete attack narrative.
  6. Attribution is Difficult: While forensics revealed the attack methodology, definitively attributing the attack to specific actors remained challenging. Attackers used infrastructure in multiple countries.

‚öñÔ∏è Legal and Regulatory Principles

  • PCI-DSS Compliance: Target violated Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard requirements for network segmentation and monitoring
  • State Data Breach Notification Laws: Target required to notify affected customers under state laws
  • Civil Liability: Forensic evidence of ignored alerts established negligence in civil lawsuits
  • Regulatory Penalties: Forensic findings led to settlements with state attorneys general
  • Shareholder Lawsuits: Forensic timeline showed when executives knew about breach vs. when they disclosed it publicly

‚öñÔ∏è Legal and Financial Outcomes

CEO Gregg Steinhafel: Resigned May 2014

CIO Beth Jacob: Resigned March 2014

Financial Settlements:

  • $18.5 million settlement with 47 states and DC
  • $10 million class action settlement with customers
  • $39.4 million settlement with banks and credit unions
  • Over $200 million in total breach-related costs

Criminal Charges: No Target employees charged criminally. However, Russian national Aleksandr Andreevich Panin was arrested and sentenced to 9.5 years for creating malware used in various breaches (not specifically Target).

üéØ Practical Takeaways

  1. Network Forensics Requires Multiple Data Sources: Combine firewall logs, IDS alerts, NetFlow data, server logs
  2. Memory Analysis for Malware: RAM-scraping malware requires memory forensics; disk analysis insufficient
  3. Third-Party Access Logging: Monitor and log all vendor/partner access to your network
  4. Alert Response Documentation: Document why alerts were dismissed or acted upon‚Äîthis becomes evidence
  5. Incident Response Integration: Forensics should inform IR; IR should preserve evidence for forensics

‚öñÔ∏è Landmark Legal Cases: Digital Forensics Precedents

Beyond criminal investigations, several court cases have established legal precedents that directly govern how digital forensic investigations must be conducted. These cases define the boundaries of legal authority and evidence admissibility.

üì± Riley v. California (2014) - Cell Phone Search Warrant Requirement ‚ñº

Facts

Police arrested David Riley for a traffic violation. During a lawful search incident to arrest, officers searched Riley's cell phone without a warrant. They found photos and videos linking Riley to a gang shooting that had occurred weeks earlier. Riley was charged with attempted murder based partly on the cell phone evidence.

Legal Issue

Question: Does the Fourth Amendment permit police to search a cell phone without a warrant when the phone is seized incident to a lawful arrest?

Background: For decades, police could search items found on arrestees without warrants under the "search incident to arrest" exception (wallets, address books, pockets). Does this exception extend to cell phones?

Supreme Court Holding

Decision: NO. Warrantless cell phone searches are unconstitutional (with limited exceptions).

Chief Justice Roberts' Reasoning:

"Modern cell phones are not just another technological convenience. With all they contain and all they may reveal, they hold for many Americans 'the privacies of life.' The fact that technology now allows an individual to carry such information in his hand does not make the information any less worthy of the protection for which the Founders fought."

Key Points:

  • Cell phones contain vast amounts of sensitive personal information
  • Quantitatively and qualitatively different from physical items like wallets
  • Privacy interests outweigh government's interest in warrantless searches
  • Officers can examine phone to ensure safety (remove battery if concerned about remote wipe)

üìñ Forensic Impact

  • Law Enforcement: Must obtain warrant before forensically examining cell phones, even after lawful arrest
  • Exigent Circumstances Exception: Still applies (e.g., bomb threat, kidnapped child, imminent danger)
  • Consent Still Valid: If arrestee voluntarily consents to search, no warrant needed
  • Protective Measures OK: Can secure phone to prevent remote wipe while obtaining warrant
  • Expert Testimony: Forensic examiners must be able to explain warrant requirement in testimony

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes Post-Riley

  • Searching phone "just to get suspect's phone number" (still requires warrant)
  • Claiming exigent circumstances without genuine emergency
  • Exceeding scope of consent ("Can I see your call log?" ‚â† permission to search entire phone)
  • Assuming Riley doesn't apply to tablets, laptops, or other devices (courts generally extend Riley to all digital devices)
üìç Carpenter v. United States (2018) - Cell Site Location Information ‚ñº

Facts

FBI obtained 127 days of Timothy Carpenter's cell site location information (CSLI) from his wireless carriers. They used a court order under the Stored Communications Act (lower standard than probable cause), not a warrant. The CSLI placed Carpenter near multiple armed robbery locations at the times of the robberies.

Legal Issue

Question: Does the government's acquisition of historical cell site location information from wireless carriers constitute a Fourth Amendment search requiring a warrant?

Government's Argument: Third-party doctrine applies‚Äîpeople voluntarily share location data with cell phone companies, so they have no reasonable expectation of privacy in that data.

Carpenter's Argument: CSLI reveals intimate details of personal movements and associations; people don't "voluntarily" share location (it's automatic and unavoidable).

Supreme Court Holding

Decision: YES. Government must obtain warrant based on probable cause for historical CSLI.

Chief Justice Roberts' Reasoning:

  • CSLI provides "all-encompassing record" of movements
  • Reveals associations, activities, and "privacies of life"
  • People don't voluntarily share location‚Äîit's automatic
  • Third-party doctrine has limits in digital age
  • 127 days of tracking is extensive surveillance

Important Limitations: Court emphasized this is a narrow decision that does NOT address:

  • Real-time CSLI or "tower dumps"
  • Conventional surveillance (security cameras)
  • Business records like bank statements
  • National security or exigent circumstances

üìñ Forensic Impact

  • Historical CSLI: Requires warrant based on probable cause (not just court order)
  • Real-Time Tracking: Legal standard still unclear; courts split on this issue
  • Other Location Data: Carpenter's implications for GPS data, IP geolocation, and app location data are still being determined by lower courts
  • Third-Party Doctrine: Not dead, but limited. Courts now consider whether data reveals intimate details of life
  • Duration Matters: Short-term location tracking may have different standard than long-term (127 days)

‚ö†Ô∏è Post-Carpenter Uncertainties

Lower courts are still working out Carpenter's boundaries:

  • Does it apply to 7 days of CSLI? 3 days? 1 day?
  • What about IP address logs that reveal location?
  • Do Google location history and Apple "Frequent Locations" require warrants?
  • What about location data from apps (Uber, fitness trackers)?

Best Practice: When in doubt, get a warrant for location data. Courts are trending toward requiring warrants for most location tracking.

‚öæ United States v. Comprehensive Drug Testing (2009) - Plain View & Digital Searches ‚ñº

Facts

Federal agents obtained a warrant to search Comprehensive Drug Testing's (CDT) records for drug test results of 10 specific Major League Baseball players suspected of steroid use. However, agents seized the entire database containing test results for 104 players‚Äîfar exceeding the warrant's scope.

The government argued that once they lawfully accessed the database, the "plain view" doctrine allowed them to seize all the data they could see.

Legal Issue

Question: Does the "plain view" doctrine justify seizing digital evidence beyond the scope specified in a search warrant?

Core Concern: If police can seize entire databases when warrant authorizes search for specific records, does this effectively convert specific warrants into general warrants (which are unconstitutional)?

Ninth Circuit Holding

Decision: Seizure violated Fourth Amendment. Plain view doctrine does not give blanket authority to seize all digital data.

The Court's Concern: Digital searches risk becoming "general warrants" that violate the Fourth Amendment's particularity requirement. The ease of seizing entire databases doesn't justify ignoring warrant limitations.

Proposed Protocols for Digital Searches:

  1. Magistrate Supervision: Warrant should specify search protocol and how to limit scope
  2. Specialized Personnel: Searches should be conducted by forensic specialists, not case agents
  3. Agreed Search Protocols: Government and defense should agree on search methodology when possible
  4. Segregation and Redaction: Separate responsive from non-responsive data; return non-responsive data
  5. Avoid Wholesale Imaging: When possible, use targeted searches rather than imaging entire systems

üìñ Forensic Impact

  • Warrant Particularity: Digital search warrants must be specific about what data is sought
  • Search Protocols: Document your search methodology to show you stayed within warrant scope
  • Taint Teams: In some cases, neutral "taint teams" review data first to separate privileged/irrelevant material
  • Return Non-Responsive Data: Don't keep data outside warrant scope "just in case"
  • Plain View Limitations: Can't use plain view as excuse to exceed warrant scope in digital searches

‚ö†Ô∏è Ongoing Controversy

The Comprehensive Drug Testing protocols are not binding law‚Äîthey were suggested guidelines. Courts disagree on how strictly to apply them. Some circuits follow them closely; others are more permissive of broad digital searches.

Best Practice: Follow the protocols as best practices even if not legally required in your jurisdiction. They demonstrate good faith and protect against suppression motions.

üíª United States v. Carey (1999) - Computer Search Warrant Scope ‚ñº

Facts

Police obtained a warrant to search James Carey's home for evidence of drug trafficking. The warrant authorized seizure of "records" related to drug activity. Officers seized Carey's computer and, during forensic examination, discovered child pornography. Carey was prosecuted for the child pornography.

Legal Issue

Question: When police lawfully search a computer for evidence of one crime (drug trafficking), can they use evidence of a different crime (child pornography) discovered during that search?

Defense Argument: Child pornography was outside the warrant's scope (which specified drug records). Evidence should be suppressed.

Government Argument: Plain view doctrine applies. Officers were lawfully searching computer and inadvertently discovered child pornography.

Tenth Circuit Holding

Decision: Evidence admissible. Plain view doctrine applies to digital searches.

Reasoning:

  • Officers were lawfully searching for drug records
  • Computers can contain many types of records; reasonable to examine various files
  • Incriminating nature of child pornography was immediately apparent
  • Discovery was inadvertent (not the target of original search)

üìñ Forensic Impact

  • Plain View Applies: Evidence of crimes outside warrant scope can be seized if discovered during lawful search
  • Document Inadvertence: Note in your report that discovery was unintentional during authorized search
  • Immediate Apparent: Incriminating nature must be clear without further analysis
  • Stop and Get New Warrant: Best practice is to stop, document discovery, and obtain new warrant for additional crimes
  • File Names Matter: Incriminating file names (e.g., "child_porn.jpg") make incriminating nature immediately apparent

‚ö†Ô∏è Tension with Comprehensive Drug Testing

Note the tension between Carey (permissive of plain view in digital searches) and Comprehensive Drug Testing (restrictive of plain view). Different circuits have different rules. Always consult local case law and legal counsel.
