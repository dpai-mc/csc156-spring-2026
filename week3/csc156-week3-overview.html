‚öñÔ∏è Laws Governing Digital Evidence ‚ñº

Federal Rules of Evidence (FRE)

  • Rule 401: Relevant Evidence - Evidence must have a tendency to make a fact more or less probable
  • Rule 403: Excluding Relevant Evidence - Court may exclude if probative value is outweighed by unfair prejudice
  • Rule 702: Expert Testimony - Qualified experts may testify if it helps the trier of fact
  • Rule 802: Hearsay Rule - Out-of-court statements generally inadmissible
  • Rule 803(6): Business Records Exception - Computer-generated records can be admitted
  • Rule 901: Authentication - Evidence must be authenticated before admission

Daubert Standard

The Daubert standard (Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, 1993) established criteria for admitting scientific expert testimony:

  • Testing: Has the theory/technique been tested?
  • Peer Review: Has it been subjected to peer review and publication?
  • Error Rate: What is the known or potential error rate?
  • Standards: Do standards and controls exist?
  • Acceptance: Is it generally accepted in the relevant scientific community?
üõ°Ô∏è Code of Ethics for Digital Forensic Investigators ‚ñº

Professional Ethics Codes

Digital forensic investigators must adhere to professional ethics codes from organizations such as:

  • IACIS (International Association of Computer Investigative Specialists)
  • HTCIA (High Technology Crime Investigation Association)
  • (ISC)¬≤ Code of Ethics
  • ISFCE (International Society of Forensic Computer Examiners)

Core Ethical Principles

  1. Objectivity: Remain neutral and unbiased; let evidence speak for itself
  2. Integrity: Be honest in all professional activities; never falsify evidence
  3. Competence: Only perform work within your expertise; seek training when needed
  4. Confidentiality: Protect sensitive information; respect privacy
  5. Accountability: Take responsibility for your work and conclusions
  6. Legal Compliance: Follow all applicable laws and regulations
üîí Privacy Considerations and Legal Boundaries ‚ñº

Fourth Amendment (U.S. Constitution)

Protects against unreasonable searches and seizures by government actors. Key concepts:

  • Reasonable Expectation of Privacy: Does the person have a legitimate privacy expectation?
  • Search Warrant Requirements: Probable cause, particular description, judicial approval
  • Private Searches Exception: Fourth Amendment doesn't apply to private parties
  • Plain View Doctrine: Evidence in plain view during lawful access may be seized

Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA)

Federal law protecting electronic communications:

  • Wiretap Act: Real-time interception of communications
  • Stored Communications Act (SCA): Stored electronic communications
  • Pen Register Act: Dialing and routing information

Other Privacy Laws

  • GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation): EU data protection and privacy
  • CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act): California data privacy rights
  • HIPAA: Health information privacy
  • FERPA: Educational records privacy

Role of an Expert Witness

Unlike fact witnesses who testify about what they observed, expert witnesses:

  • Provide specialized knowledge to help the court understand technical evidence
  • Offer opinions and interpretations based on their expertise
  • Explain complex technical concepts in understandable terms
  • May be asked hypothetical questions

Qualifying as an Expert

Courts consider: Education, Training, Experience, Publications, Certifications, Previous testimony

Testimony Best Practices

Before Testimony:

  • Review case materials thoroughly
  • Meet with attorney to understand case strategy
  • Prepare visual aids (diagrams, screenshots, timelines)
  • Anticipate challenges and weaknesses
  • Practice through mock cross-examination

During Testimony:

  • Dress professionally (business attire)
  • Speak clearly; face the jury, not the attorney
  • Pause before answering (gives attorney time to object)
  • Avoid absolutes; use "In my opinion," "Based on my analysis"
  • Admit limitations honestly; enhances credibility
