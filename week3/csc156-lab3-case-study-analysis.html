üìñ Part 1: Case Law Analysis (10 points)

Part 1 | 10 Points

Instructions

Select TWO of the following landmark cases and provide a comprehensive analysis of each. Each case analysis should be 1-2 pages.

Choose TWO Cases from This List:

Option 1: United States v. Carey (1999)

Topic: Computer search warrant scope and plain view doctrine

Key Issue: Can evidence of crimes outside warrant scope be seized during lawful computer search?

Option 2: United States v. Comprehensive Drug Testing (2009)

Topic: Plain view doctrine limitations in digital searches

Key Issue: Does plain view doctrine justify seizing entire databases when warrant specifies limited records?

Option 3: Riley v. California (2014)

Topic: Cell phone searches incident to arrest

Key Issue: Does Fourth Amendment require warrant to search cell phones after lawful arrest?

Option 4: Carpenter v. United States (2018)

Topic: Cell site location information and privacy expectations

Key Issue: Does government need warrant to obtain historical cell site location data from carriers?

üìù Required Analysis for Each Case (5 points each)

For each of your TWO selected cases, provide:

  1. Facts (0.5 points): Brief summary of what happened
    • Who were the parties involved?
    • What was the criminal or civil matter?
    • What digital evidence was at issue?
    • Length: 2-3 sentences
  2. Legal Issue (1 point): What constitutional or evidentiary question was at stake?
    • Frame as a specific legal question
    • Identify which amendment, statute, or rule is involved
    • Explain why this question matters
    • Length: 3-4 sentences
  3. Holding (1.5 points): How did the court rule?
    • State the court's decision clearly
    • Explain the court's reasoning
    • Include key quotes from the opinion if available
    • Note any limitations or exceptions the court mentioned
    • Length: 1 paragraph (5-7 sentences)
  4. Forensic Implications (1.5 points): How does this case affect digital forensics practice?
    • What must forensic examiners do differently because of this case?
    • What procedures or policies changed?
    • How does this affect evidence admissibility?
    • What challenges does this create for investigators?
    • Length: 1-2 paragraphs (6-10 sentences)
  5. Personal Analysis (0.5 points): Do you agree with the court's reasoning? Why or why not?
    • State your position clearly
    • Provide reasoned justification
    • Consider competing interests (privacy vs. law enforcement)
    • Length: 3-5 sentences

üîç Part 2: Scenario Analysis (10 points)

Part 2 | 10 Points

Instructions

Analyze the following investigation scenario for legal and ethical issues. Identify problems, explain the relevant legal principles, and recommend proper procedures.

üìÅ Scenario: "The Departing Employee"

Background:

TechCorp suspects that Sarah Martinez, a software engineer who recently resigned, has stolen proprietary source code. The company's acceptable use policy states that all data on company systems is subject to monitoring and that employees have no expectation of privacy.

On Sarah's last day, the IT director makes a forensic image of her laptop before she returns it. During examination, you discover:

  1. Company Source Code: Multiple repositories cloned to external USB drive (confirmed by file system artifacts showing USB connection at 2:15 PM on her last day)
  2. Personal Gmail Account: Sarah accessed her personal Gmail through the browser. You find:
    • Emails to her personal account with attachments named "TechCorp_API_Documentation.pdf"
    • Draft emails to a competitor discussing employment opportunities
    • Personal medical records (emails from her doctor about a serious health condition)
    • Attorney-client communications about her upcoming divorce
  3. Encrypted Container: A VeraCrypt encrypted container (500 MB) with unknown password. File creation date: one week before resignation.
  4. Browser History: Searches for:
    • "How to securely delete files"
    • "Can employers see personal email"
    • "Statute of limitations trade secret theft California"

Additional Context:

  • Sarah is a California resident
  • TechCorp has customers in the European Union
  • This is a corporate investigation, not law enforcement
  • Sarah has not been informed of the investigation
  • The laptop is company-owned

üìù Required Analysis (10 points total)

Your analysis must address the following:

  1. Legal Authority (2 points):
    • Does TechCorp have legal authority to image and examine Sarah's laptop?
    • Does the Fourth Amendment apply to this corporate search?
    • What role does the acceptable use policy play?
    • Are there any limits to TechCorp's search authority?
  2. Privacy Considerations (2 points):
    • What privacy issues arise from examining Sarah's personal Gmail?
    • How should you handle the medical records discovered?
    • What about the attorney-client communications regarding her divorce?
    • Does CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act) apply?
  3. Ethical Issues (2 points):
    • What ethical principles are implicated? (Reference specific ethics codes)
    • How do you balance thoroughness with privacy respect?
    • Should you examine the encrypted container? Why or why not?
    • What should you include/exclude in your report?
  4. Evidence Admissibility (2 points):
    • Which evidence would likely be admissible if this goes to court?
    • What FRE rules apply (relevance, authentication, hearsay)?
    • Could any evidence be excluded? On what grounds?
    • How does chain of custody factor in?
  5. Recommendations (2 points):
    • What should the forensic examiner do next?
    • What data should be included in the report?
    • What data should be excluded or handled specially?
    • Should law enforcement be contacted? When and why?
    • What safeguards should be implemented for sensitive data?

Length: 2-3 pages for scenario analysis

üí≠ Critical Thinking Questions to Consider

  • What if Sarah claims she didn't know personal email would be examined?
  • How does attorney-client privilege affect your examination?
  • What if the encrypted container contains only personal data?
  • How would your analysis change if this were a law enforcement investigation?
  • What if Sarah is an EU citizen and GDPR applies?
  • Should you attempt to crack the encrypted container?
