Week 8

Windows Forensics II: Event Logs and System Artifacts

CSC 156 – Digital Forensics Fundamentals

Welcome to Week 8

Welcome back! Last week you explored the Windows Registry and user-generated artifacts such as RecentDocs, UserAssist, and Jump Lists. This week you shift focus to system-level and OS-generated artifacts—Windows Event Logs, program-execution evidence, shortcut (LNK) files, Shellbags, Volume Shadow Copies, and NTFS journal structures. Together, Weeks 7 and 8 give you a comprehensive toolkit for examining Windows systems in a forensic investigation.

The week culminates with an introduction to super-timeline construction, where you merge multiple artifact sources into a single chronological view—one of the most powerful techniques in digital forensics. You will also begin preparing for the Midterm Exam in Week 9, which covers all material from Weeks 1–8.

What You Will Learn This Week

By the end of Week 8, you should feel confident in your ability to:

  • Read and interpret Windows Event Logs—especially Security, System, and Application channels—to spot logon activity, account changes, and service events.
  • Identify evidence that a specific program was executed on a Windows machine using Prefetch files, AmCache, and ShimCache artifacts.
  • Analyze LNK (shortcut) files and Shellbag entries to reconstruct where a user browsed and what files they accessed.
  • Understand how Volume Shadow Copies work and how examiners use them to recover earlier versions of files.
  • Parse NTFS journal artifacts ($LogFile and $UsnJrnl) and detect timestamp manipulation (timestomping).
  • Combine all of these artifact types into an integrated super-timeline that tells a coherent investigative story.

Looking Ahead

Week 9 and beyond

The Midterm Exam takes place in Week 9 and covers everything from Weeks 1–8. The timeline-reconstruction and artifact-correlation skills you build this week are directly tested on the exam, so treat Module 8.5's review content as essential preparation.

After the midterm, Week 9 also introduces Linux Forensics, where you will compare Windows Event Logs with Linux syslog and journald—a natural extension of what you learn this week. The investigative mindset and multi-artifact correlation approach you practice now will carry through the rest of the course, including Memory Forensics (Week 10), Network Forensics (Weeks 11–12), and your Final Project report (Week 17).
