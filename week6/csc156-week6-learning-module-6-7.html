🎯 Module Overview

📋 What You'll Learn

Documentation is the foundation of legally defensible forensic work. This final module of Week 6 brings together all acquisition concepts into comprehensive documentation practices that support your findings from collection through court presentation.

According to NIST SP 800-86: "A clearly defined chain of custody should be followed to avoid allegations of mishandling or tampering of evidence. This involves keeping a log of every person who had physical custody of the evidence, documenting the actions that they performed on the evidence and at what time."

✓ Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Identify all required elements of acquisition documentation
  • Complete acquisition logs according to professional standards
  • Create photographic documentation of the acquisition process
  • Prepare documentation suitable for legal proceedings
  • Avoid common documentation errors that could compromise cases

⚠️ The Documentation Imperative

"If it isn't documented, it didn't happen."

In forensic investigations, undocumented actions are legally equivalent to actions that never occurred. Defense attorneys will challenge any gap in documentation, potentially rendering evidence inadmissible.

📁 Essential Documentation Types

Forensic acquisition requires multiple types of documentation, each serving a specific purpose in establishing evidence integrity and legal admissibility.

📝
Acquisition Log
Detailed record of the imaging process including tools, settings, times, and issues encountered.
When: During and immediately after acquisition
🔗
Chain of Custody
Chronological record of every person who handled the evidence, when, and why.
When: From collection through final disposition
🔐
Hash Verification Log
Record of cryptographic hash values for source and image, with verification results.
When: Before, during, and after acquisition
📸
Photographic Evidence
Photos documenting evidence condition, serial numbers, connections, and physical state.
When: Before, during, and after handling
🛠️
Tool Documentation
Record of all forensic tools used, including software versions and hardware serial numbers.
When: Document at time of use
⚠️
Error/Anomaly Log
Documentation of any errors or unexpected conditions and how they were addressed.
When: Immediately when issues occur

ℹ️ Contemporaneous Documentation

Contemporaneous means documented at the time of the action, not afterward from memory. Courts give greater weight to contemporaneous notes because memory fades and real-time documentation captures details that may seem unimportant later.

📝 The Acquisition Log

Acquisition Log

A comprehensive record documenting all aspects of the forensic imaging process, including evidence identification, tools and methods used, timing, hash values, verification results, and any issues encountered.

Required Elements

Required elements of acquisition log
Category Required Information
Case Information Case number, evidence item number, investigation name, date/time with timezone
Evidence Description Device type, make, model, serial number, capacity, physical condition
Examiner Information Full name, badge/ID number, organization, contact information
Acquisition Details Start/end times, location, acquisition type, image format, compression
Tools Used Imaging software (name/version), write blocker (make/model/serial), workstation
Hash Values Source hash (MD5, SHA-256), image hash, verification result
Errors/Anomalies Any errors encountered, bad sectors, resolution steps taken

🔗 Chain of Custody for Acquisition

💡 Review from Week 2

Chain of custody was introduced in Week 2, Module 4. This section focuses specifically on chain of custody requirements during the acquisition phase. Key principles:

  • Document every transfer of custody
  • Record who, what, when, where, why, and how
  • Maintain continuous documentation—no gaps
  • Use tamper-evident seals and document seal status

Acquisition-Specific Chain of Custody Elements

Chain of custody elements specific to acquisition
Element What to Document
Receipt of Evidence When evidence was received, from whom, seal condition
Seal Break When, why, and by whom seal was broken; witness if present
Acquisition Activity What was done to the evidence during acquisition
Resealing New seal number applied, initials, date/time
Return/Storage Where evidence was returned or stored after acquisition
Digital Copy Created Note that forensic image was created, storage location, hash values

⚠️ Common Chain of Custody Errors

  • Missing signatures: Both parties must sign every transfer
  • Incomplete descriptions: Include make, model, serial number
  • Time gaps: Every minute must be accounted for
  • Undocumented seal breaks: Always document when and why
  • Vague locations: Specify room number, locker, etc.

⚖️ Preparing Documentation for Court

Forensic documentation must be prepared with the assumption that it will be presented in court and scrutinized by opposing counsel.

Anticipating Cross-Examination

Proper Response:

"I calculated cryptographic hash values of both the source device and the forensic image using SHA-256. The hash values match exactly, which mathematically proves the image is a bit-for-bit identical copy. This is documented in my acquisition log."

Supporting Documentation:

  • Hash verification log with source and image hashes
  • Acquisition log showing verification was performed

Proper Response:

"I used a validated hardware write blocker which physically prevents any write commands from reaching the evidence drive. The write blocker was validated on [date] and has been NIST tested."

Supporting Documentation:

  • Write blocker make, model, serial number
  • Write blocker validation records
  • NIST test reports

Proper Response:

"Yes. The chain of custody documentation shows continuous custody from the moment I received the evidence until I returned it to storage. Every transfer is documented with signatures from both parties."

Supporting Documentation:

  • Complete chain of custody form
  • No time gaps in documentation
  • Signatures from all parties

Proper Response (if errors occurred):

"Yes, the acquisition log documents [X] read errors at sectors [locations]. These were handled using the conv=noerror,sync option, which filled those sectors with zeros. The affected data represents [percentage] of the total drive and the locations are documented."

Key Point: Never hide errors. Documented errors with explanations are far better than undocumented issues discovered later.

🔍 Scenario: Undocumented Evidence Transfer

Situation: You receive an external hard drive from a police officer for imaging. The officer hands it to you, you image it, and then hand it back. You update your acquisition log but forget to update the chain of custody form for both the receipt and return of the evidence.

Months later, the case goes to court. The defense attorney asks, "Can you show me where the chain of custody documents the transfer of this evidence from Officer Smith to you, and then back to him?"
