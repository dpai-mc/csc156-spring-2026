What Is Incident Response?

Incident Response (IR) is the organized approach to addressing and managing the aftermath of a security breach or cyberattack. Its goal is to handle the situation in a way that limits damage, reduces recovery time and costs, and preserves evidence for potential legal proceedings.

Key Distinctions

Three related but distinct disciplines often get conflated. Understanding where each begins and ends is critical for the forensic examiner:

DisciplinePrimary FocusTimeframe Emphasis
Incident ResponseStrategic coordination of detection, containment, eradication, and recovery across the organizationReal-time through post-incident
Incident HandlingTactical, procedural execution of response steps (triage, communication, escalation)Real-time / operational
Digital ForensicsMethodical collection, preservation, analysis, and presentation of digital evidenceDuring and after the incident; may extend months for legal proceedings

💡 Big Picture

Digital forensics supports incident response — it is not a substitute for it. A forensic examiner who understands the full IR lifecycle can anticipate what evidence will be needed, when to collect it, and how containment decisions may alter or destroy artifacts.

Why Structured IR Matters

  • Regulatory compliance: Frameworks like HIPAA (healthcare), PCI-DSS (payment cards), and GDPR (EU data) mandate documented incident response plans with specific notification timelines.
  • Legal admissibility: Evidence collected outside a structured process may be challenged in court. Chain of custody begins the moment the first responder touches a system.
  • Business continuity: Organizations with tested IR plans recover 58% faster on average (Ponemon Institute, 2024). Downtime costs in healthcare can exceed $9,000 per minute.
  • Insurance requirements: Cyber insurance carriers increasingly require a documented IR plan and evidence of tabletop exercises as a condition of coverage.

⚠️ Real-World Consequence

In 2023, a healthcare system that lacked a structured IR plan paid $4.5 million in HIPAA fines — not because of the breach itself, but because their chaotic response failed to meet the 60-day notification window and could not demonstrate what patient data was actually accessed.

NIST SP 800-61: The Four-Phase Lifecycle

The National Institute of Standards and Technology's Computer Security Incident Handling Guide (SP 800-61, Rev. 2) defines the most widely adopted IR lifecycle in the United States. It organizes response into four phases connected by a continuous feedback loop.

Preparation is everything the organization does before an incident occurs. It is the only proactive phase.

  • Develop and maintain the IR policy, plan, and playbooks
  • Build and train the IR team; assign roles and on-call rotations
  • Deploy detection infrastructure (SIEM, EDR, network sensors)
  • Establish communication channels (secure out-of-band comms, legal contacts, PR templates)
  • Conduct tabletop exercises and purple-team drills
  • Prepare forensic jump kits: write-blockers, acquisition media, validated tool images
  • Maintain asset inventories and network diagrams (critical for scoping)

Forensic examiner focus: Validate tool readiness, ensure imaging media is wiped and hashed, confirm chain-of-custody forms are current, and verify legal authority/consent documentation templates are accessible.

This phase encompasses identifying that an incident has occurred, determining its scope, and prioritizing the response.

  • Detection sources: SIEM alerts, IDS/IPS, EDR, user reports, threat intelligence feeds, law enforcement notification
  • Initial triage: Is this a true positive? What is the severity and scope?
  • Analysis activities: Log correlation, indicator of compromise (IOC) matching, timeline reconstruction, affected-system identification
  • Documentation: Incident ticket creation, initial evidence log, communication to stakeholders

Forensic examiner focus: Begin evidence preservation planning. Identify volatile data sources at risk. Advise on which systems need immediate imaging vs. live triage. Start the chain-of-custody log.

✅ Examiner Tip

At MedTech, the forensic examiner's first question upon notification should be: "Is that radiology server still powered on, and who has accessed it since the alert?" Volatile memory evidence disappears the moment the system is shut down or rebooted.

This combined phase addresses stopping the spread, removing the threat, and restoring normal operations.

Containment

  • Short-term: Network isolation, disabling compromised accounts, firewall blocks
  • Long-term: Patching, system hardening, deploying additional monitoring before bringing systems back
  • Evidence preservation: Image systems before eradication when possible

Eradication

  • Remove malware, backdoors, and unauthorized accounts
  • Identify and remediate root cause (unpatched vulnerability, phished credential, etc.)
  • Verify eradication through scanning and monitoring

Recovery

  • Restore systems from known-good backups or rebuild
  • Gradual reconnection with enhanced monitoring
  • Validate system integrity before returning to production

Forensic examiner focus: Containment decisions directly impact evidence. The examiner must communicate clearly: "I need 45 minutes to image this server's RAM and disk before you re-image it." Coordination with the IR lead is essential to balance business urgency against evidentiary needs.

Often called "Lessons Learned," this phase closes the loop and drives continuous improvement.

  • Lessons-learned meeting: What worked? What failed? What would we do differently?
  • Final incident report: Complete timeline, root cause analysis, impact assessment, recommendations
  • Evidence retention: Secure storage per retention policy and legal hold requirements
  • Metrics collection: Time to detect, time to contain, total impact cost
  • Plan updates: Revise IR plan, playbooks, and training based on findings

Forensic examiner focus: Finalize the forensic report, ensure all evidence is catalogued and stored with maintained chain of custody, support legal counsel with findings, and recommend detection signatures based on artifacts discovered.

💡 Big Picture

Neither model is "better." NIST is more common in government and regulated industries; SANS is popular in private-sector training and certifications (GCIH, GCFE). Most real-world IR plans borrow from both. As a forensic examiner, your job maps the same regardless of which framework the organization uses — what changes is the vocabulary your teammates use.

Other Frameworks at a Glance

📝 Framework Survey

  • ISO/IEC 27035 (Information Security Incident Management): International standard providing a five-phase model (Plan & Prepare, Detection & Reporting, Assessment & Decision, Responses, Lessons Learned); commonly required for organizations seeking ISO 27001 certification.
  • CISA Incident Response Playbooks: U.S. federal playbooks (2021) aligned with Executive Order 14028; provide step-by-step procedures for specific incident types (e.g., ransomware, supply-chain compromise) and are mandatory for federal civilian agencies.
  • MITRE ATT&CK Framework: Not an IR lifecycle per se, but a knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques that IR teams use during Detection & Analysis to map attacker behavior and predict next moves.
  • Industry-specific requirements: HIPAA (healthcare, 60-day notification), PCI-DSS (payment card, 72-hour notification to acquirer), NERC CIP (energy sector, 1-hour reporting for certain events), and state breach notification laws (varying timelines, typically 30–72 hours).

IR Team Structure & The Forensic Examiner's Role

Effective incident response requires a multidisciplinary team. Below is a typical Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT) structure:

  • IR Manager / Incident Commander: Overall authority; makes containment and escalation decisions; coordinates cross-functional communication.
  • Triage Analyst / SOC Analyst: First-line detection and initial classification; escalates confirmed incidents.
  • Forensic Examiner: Evidence collection, preservation, analysis, and reporting (detailed below).
  • Threat Intelligence Analyst: Provides context on adversary TTPs, IOCs, and attribution.
  • Malware Analyst / Reverse Engineer: Analyzes malicious code to understand capabilities, persistence mechanisms, and C2 infrastructure.
  • Systems / Network Administrator: Implements containment actions, provides system access, assists with recovery.
  • Communications Lead: Manages internal and external messaging, coordinates with PR and legal.
  • Legal Counsel: Advises on regulatory obligations, privilege, evidence handling, and liability. Often determines whether law enforcement is contacted.
  • Law Enforcement (FBI, Secret Service, local cyber units): May assist with attribution, provide threat intelligence, or take over investigation for prosecution. Contact is typically a business decision made by legal/executive leadership — not the forensic examiner unilaterally.
  • External DFIR Firms: Retained (often via cyber insurance panel) for surge capacity, specialized expertise, or independent third-party validation.
  • Regulatory Bodies: HHS/OCR for HIPAA, state AG offices, EU DPAs for GDPR — notification recipients, not active responders.
  • Cyber Insurance Carrier: Must be notified early (often within 24–72 hours); may dictate which DFIR firm and legal counsel are used (panel providers). Failure to notify promptly can void coverage.

What the Forensic Examiner DOES:

  • Captures and preserves volatile evidence (RAM, running processes, network connections)
  • Creates forensically sound disk images using validated tools and write-blockers
  • Maintains rigorous chain-of-custody documentation
  • Analyzes artifacts: file systems, logs, registry, browser history, malware remnants
  • Constructs detailed timelines of attacker activity
  • Produces written reports suitable for legal, regulatory, and executive audiences
  • Advises the IR team on evidence implications of containment decisions
  • Testifies as an expert witness if the case proceeds to litigation or prosecution

What the Forensic Examiner Does NOT Typically Do:

  • Make unilateral containment or eradication decisions (that authority belongs to the IR Manager)
  • Contact law enforcement independently (legal counsel makes that call)
  • Notify regulators or affected individuals (compliance/legal function)
  • Issue public statements or communicate with media
  • Perform system recovery or rebuild production systems (sysadmin role)
  • Make business-risk decisions about whether to pay ransoms or accept downtime

✅ Professional Boundary

The forensic examiner's superpower is objectivity. By staying in the evidence lane and not making operational decisions, the examiner's findings remain credible and defensible. The moment you start making containment calls, your impartiality as an analyst can be questioned in court.
