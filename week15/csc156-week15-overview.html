Week 15: Incident Response Frameworks and Capstone Preparation

CSC 156 – Digital Forensic Fundamentals

This week is a pivotal transition point in the course. You will learn how all of the individual forensic skills you have built over the past fourteen weeks fit within a structured incident response process — and you will launch the Final Project (Capstone), your opportunity to execute a complete forensic investigation from evidence intake through professional reporting.

Where Week 15 Fits

Recently completed: In Week 13 you performed static and dynamic malware analysis and extracted Indicators of Compromise. In Week 14 you studied the anatomy of a professional forensic report, documentation standards, and expert testimony preparation.

This week: You shift from "How do I analyze evidence?" to "How does forensic analysis fit within an organization's incident response process?" — and you begin your capstone investigation.

Coming next: Week 16 covers Mobile and Cloud Forensics, introducing mobile device acquisition and cloud service log analysis while you continue capstone work.

Week 15 Learning Goals

By the end of this week you should be able to:

  1. Describe the phases of an industry-standard incident response lifecycle (NIST SP 800-61 or SANS PICERL) and explain where forensic activities occur in each phase.
  2. Explain team roles in incident response and how a forensic examiner interacts with other stakeholders.
  3. Map specific forensic techniques from Weeks 2–14 to the appropriate IR lifecycle phases.
  4. Apply triage and prioritization principles to determine which evidence to examine first under time pressure.
  5. Develop an incident response plan outline that integrates forensic acquisition, analysis, and reporting.
  6. Understand the capstone project requirements, deliverables, timeline, and evaluation criteria.

Key Concepts This Week

By the end of Week 15, make sure you can explain each of these:

  • NIST SP 800-61 four-phase IR lifecycle
  • SANS PICERL model and its mapping to NIST phases
  • Containment vs. Eradication vs. Recovery (and forensic considerations for each)
  • Order of Volatility and triage prioritization
  • Forensic readiness and preparation-phase planning
  • IR team roles — especially the forensic examiner's responsibilities and handoffs
  • IR playbooks and tabletop exercises as planning tools

Total estimated time: 11–13 hours (including lab completion).

  • Think integrative: This week rewards students who can connect concepts across multiple prior weeks. Review your notes from Weeks 6, 7–9, 11, 12, and 13 — you will map those techniques to IR phases.
  • Use the frameworks as organizers: The NIST and SANS models give you a mental filing system. When studying, ask "Which IR phase does this skill belong to?"
  • Start your capstone plan early: Even though the final report is not due until Week 17, reading the scenario and outlining your approach on Day 5 will save significant time later.
  • Lab 14 is collaborative in nature: The tabletop exercise asks you to think through a scenario step by step. Approach it like a real incident — document your reasoning, not just your answers.
  • Revisit Week 14: The forensic report structure you learned last week is exactly the format your capstone deliverable will follow.

Looking Ahead: Week 16

Plan to balance new mobile/cloud content with steady capstone progress throughout Week 16.
