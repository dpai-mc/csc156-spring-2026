Why a Tabletop Format?

💡 Understanding the Assessment Design

Tabletop exercises are a standard professional practice in Digital Forensics and Incident Response (DFIR). This format was chosen deliberately to assess higher-order thinking skills that tool-based labs cannot measure.

Professional Relevance

In real-world DFIR practice, tabletop exercises serve critical functions:

  • Team readiness testing — Organizations run tabletops to validate their IR plans before real incidents occur.
  • Decision-making under ambiguity — Real incidents rarely present clean, obvious paths. Tabletops train practitioners to reason through incomplete information.
  • Interview preparation — DFIR job interviews frequently present candidates with scenario-based questions: "You receive an alert that… What do you do first? Why?"

Higher-Order Thinking Skills Assessed

Lab 14 moves beyond recall and application into analysis, evaluation, and synthesis:

  • Analysis — Breaking the scenario into phases and identifying what type of IR activity is occurring at each stage.
  • Evaluation — Judging which forensic actions are appropriate (and which are not) given the current phase, available resources, and time constraints.
  • Synthesis — Constructing an original IR plan outline that integrates multiple forensic techniques into a coherent, sequenced strategy.

What This Means for You

There is no single "correct answer" for every decision point. Your responses will be evaluated on the quality of your reasoning, your use of precise framework terminology, and how well you justify your choices using concepts from Modules 15.1–15.3.

Task Preview

Lab 14 consists of four tasks. Each task builds on the previous one, progressing from identification through planning — mirroring how a forensic examiner moves through an actual incident engagement.

Task 1: IR Phase Identification

7 points

You will be presented with a series of scenario snapshots describing activities occurring during an incident. For each snapshot, you must identify the current IR phase (using NIST or SANS terminology) and provide a brief justification explaining why the described activity belongs to that phase.

Prepare by: Ensuring you can clearly distinguish between Detection & Analysis, Containment, Eradication, and Recovery activities — especially the boundary cases where phases overlap.

Task 2: Forensic Action Selection

8 points

At several decision points in the scenario, you must select appropriate forensic actions from a set of options and explain your reasoning. This tests your ability to match specific tools and techniques (from Weeks 2–14) to the current IR phase, available evidence sources, and scenario constraints.

Prepare by: Reviewing the technique-to-phase mapping from Module 15.2. Know which tools apply to which evidence types and which phase they belong in.

Task 3: Triage Prioritization

7 points

Given a list of available evidence sources and a time constraint, you must rank the evidence in collection priority order and justify your ranking using the order of volatility, severity/scope analysis, and business impact considerations from Module 15.3.

Prepare by: Practicing the prioritization decision matrix (severity × scope × volatility). Be ready to explain why memory comes before disk, and why some disk artifacts might leapfrog others based on scenario context.

Task 4: IR Plan Outline

8 points

You will develop a structured IR plan outline for the scenario organization. The plan must include: roles and responsibilities, communication procedures, forensic acquisition sequence, escalation criteria, and evidence handling procedures. This is a synthesis task requiring you to integrate everything from Modules 15.1–15.3.

Prepare by: Reviewing the IR plan components from Module 15.3 (Section 5.1 of the detailed outline). Your plan should be realistic for the scenario organization — consider their size, industry, and regulatory requirements.

Total: 30 points — Note how point values increase slightly for tasks requiring greater synthesis and original thinking.

Tips for Success

🚀 Maximize Your Lab 14 Score

These strategies reflect what distinguishes strong responses from average ones in professional DFIR tabletop exercises — and in this lab's rubric.

  1. Use precise framework terminology. Say "Containment phase (NIST)" or "Identification phase (SANS PICERL)" — not "the part where you stop the attack." Graders look for correct, specific phase names.
  2. Reference specific tools and artifact paths. Instead of "collect logs," write "collect Windows Security Event Logs (Security.evtx) using KAPE" or "capture volatile memory with FTK Imager before network isolation." Specificity demonstrates competence.
  3. Justify every prioritization decision. Don't just list a collection order — explain why. Reference volatility (RFC 3227), relevance to the specific attack type, or regulatory requirements that create deadlines.
  4. Make your IR plan realistic for the scenario organization. A 50-person healthcare company has different resources than a Fortune 500 firm. Tailor roles, escalation paths, and external contacts to the scenario's organizational context.
  5. Address forensic considerations at phase boundaries. The strongest responses identify what must happen forensically before transitioning between phases (e.g., "capture memory before containment isolation cuts network access").
  6. Write clearly and concisely. This is a written assessment. Organize your responses with headings, bullet points, or numbered steps. Graders should not have to hunt for your reasoning.
