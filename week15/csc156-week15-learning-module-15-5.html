Project Description & Your Role

The Scenario

You are a digital forensic examiner engaged by a mid-size organization ("Meridian Health Systems") that has experienced a confirmed security incident. The organization's internal incident response team has already:

  • Detected anomalous network activity and confirmed unauthorized access.
  • Performed initial containment (isolated affected hosts, preserved volatile evidence).
  • Collected forensic evidence packages and delivered them to you for analysis.

Your Mission

Using the four evidence packages provided (disk image, memory dump, PCAP, and log files), you must:

  1. Analyze all evidence sources using appropriate forensic tools and methodology.
  2. Reconstruct the full incident timeline from initial compromise through attacker activity.
  3. Produce a professional forensic report suitable for legal counsel and executive leadership.
  4. Present your findings orally to a simulated audience of technical and non-technical stakeholders.

Six Core Investigation Questions

Your analysis must answer each of the following:

#Investigation Question
1Initial Access: How did the attacker gain entry to the environment? What vulnerability or vector was exploited?
2Attacker Actions: What did the attacker do on the initially compromised host? What tools or commands were executed?
3Lateral Movement: Did the attacker move to other systems? What evidence supports (or refutes) lateral movement?
4Data Compromise: Was any sensitive data accessed, exfiltrated, or destroyed? What evidence supports your conclusion?
5Persistence: Did the attacker establish mechanisms for continued access? Identify all persistence artifacts found.
6Recommendations: Based on your findings, what specific remediation and hardening actions should the organization take?

Format Requirements

  • Length: 12–20 pages (excluding appendices)
  • Format: PDF submission
  • Evidence support: Every factual claim must reference a specific artifact (exhibit number, file path, hash, or screenshot)
  • Chain of custody: Include a completed chain of custody form documenting your receipt and handling of evidence
  • Language: Professional, objective, factual — avoid speculation not supported by evidence

B. Oral Presentation (Secondary Deliverable — Week 17)

  • Duration: 10–12 minutes
  • Audience: Simulated mix of technical staff and executive leadership
  • Format: Slide-based presentation (tool of your choice)
  • Q&A: 3–5 minutes of audience questions following your presentation
  • Focus: Summarize the incident narrative, key evidence, impact, and recommendations — do not simply read your report aloud

Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

Students in prior semesters have encountered these challenges. Awareness now will save you time and points later.

1. Starting Without a Plan

Fix: Complete the analysis plan above before opening any evidence files. Know what you are looking for and in what order.

2. Focusing on One Evidence Source

Fix: All four evidence types are provided for a reason. Cross-correlate findings across memory, disk, network, and logs to build a complete picture.

3. Reporting Tool Output Without Interpretation

Fix: Raw output is evidence, not analysis. Always explain what the artifact means, why it matters, and how it connects to the incident narrative.

4. Missing the "So What?"

Fix: For every finding, ask: "What does this mean for the organization?" Connect technical artifacts to business impact and investigation questions.

5. Delaying Report Writing

Fix: Write as you analyze. Draft findings sections during Week 16 — do not leave all writing for the final days of Week 17.

6. Neglecting Chain of Custody

Fix: Document evidence receipt and every handling action from the moment you access the files. A missing chain of custody form costs points and undermines credibility.

Knowledge Check

These questions are ungraded and designed to confirm your understanding of project expectations before you begin.

Question 1: Before you open any evidence files for analysis, what steps should you complete first?

Correct! Before any analysis begins, you must verify evidence integrity (hash comparison), document receipt on your chain of custody form, and create a structured analysis plan. This ensures your work is forensically sound and defensible — and it directly supports the Methodology and Evidence Documentation rubric criteria (22 combined points).
Not quite. The correct answer is B. Before opening evidence, you must: (1) verify hash values to confirm evidence integrity, (2) complete the chain of custody form documenting your receipt, and (3) develop an analysis plan. Skipping these steps compromises forensic soundness and costs you points on the Methodology (12 pts) and Evidence Documentation (10 pts) rubric criteria.

Question 2: A student's report contains technically accurate findings (full 15 pts for Technical Accuracy) but receives only 3 of 8 points for Timeline Reconstruction. Which of the following most likely explains the low timeline score?

Correct! Timeline Reconstruction (8 pts) specifically evaluates your ability to present events in chronological sequence with UTC timestamps and cross-source correlation. A report can be technically accurate yet still fail to reconstruct the narrative timeline if findings are siloed by evidence type rather than woven into a coherent chronological story.
Not quite. The correct answer is B. The Timeline Reconstruction criterion evaluates chronological sequencing and cross-source correlation — not tool choice, report length, or section completeness. If findings are presented per evidence source without merging them into a unified timeline, the student loses most timeline points even if the underlying analysis is correct.
