Week-by-Week Review — Weeks 1–8

💡 How to Use This Section

Expand each week to review the most testable concepts and complete one ungraded self-check question. The self-check questions are representative of the types of questions you will encounter on the Midterm Exam. Work through every week — even topics that feel familiar.

Most Testable Concepts

  • Definition of digital forensics: The application of scientific methods to collect, preserve, analyze, and present digital evidence in a legally admissible manner.
  • Role in incident response: Forensics supports IR by providing evidence of what happened, how, when, and by whom — after containment is initiated.
  • Professional certifications: IACIS CFCE, GIAC GCFE, GIAC GCFA, EC-Council CHFI — know which organization issues each and their general focus areas.
  • CSLO 1 anchor week: Week 1 establishes the foundational vocabulary for the entire course. Exam questions may ask you to define terms or distinguish between concepts introduced here.
  • Career context: Digital forensics practitioners work in law enforcement, corporate security, incident response, legal support, and consulting.

🔎 Self-Check — Week 1

Which of the following best describes the primary distinction between digital forensics and general IT troubleshooting?

Most Testable Concepts

  • Six investigation phases: Identification → Preservation → Collection/Acquisition → Examination → Analysis → Presentation. Know each phase's purpose and key actions.
  • Forensic readiness: Proactive organizational capability to maximize evidence quality while minimizing investigation costs. Includes logging strategies, trained staff, and evidence handling procedures.
  • Chain of custody: Chronological documentation of who handled evidence, when, where, and how. Required for evidence admissibility. Every transfer must be documented.
  • Order of volatility: CPU/cache → RAM → Network connections → Running processes → Temporary files → Disk → Remote logs. Always collect most volatile evidence first.
  • Documentation standards: Contemporaneous, objective, complete. Notes may become evidence themselves. Never erase — cross out and initial corrections.

🔎 Self-Check — Week 2

An investigator arrives at a scene and discovers a running workstation suspected of involvement in a data breach. Following the order of volatility, what should be captured FIRST?

Most Testable Concepts

  • Federal Rules of Evidence: Digital evidence must be relevant, authentic, and not unfairly prejudicial. Authentication requires proving the evidence is what it claims to be.
  • Daubert Standard: Federal test for admissibility of expert testimony — must be based on sufficient facts, reliable methods, and applied reliably to the case.
  • Fourth Amendment: Protects against unreasonable government searches. Applies to government actors; private investigators are generally not bound by it. Warrants require probable cause.
  • Code of ethics: Forensic examiners must be objective, impartial, and report findings accurately — including exculpatory evidence. Personal bias must not influence findings.
  • Privacy laws: ECPA, GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA — each imposes restrictions on data collection and handling. Know the general scope of each.
  • Expert witness: Must be qualified by knowledge, skill, experience, training, or education. Provides opinion testimony, not just factual testimony.

🔎 Self-Check — Week 3

A corporate security investigator — not a law enforcement officer — searches an employee's company-owned laptop without a warrant. Which of the following is most accurate regarding the Fourth Amendment?

Most Testable Concepts

  • HDD vs. SSD forensics: HDDs allow deleted data recovery from magnetic platters; SSDs use TRIM, wear leveling, and garbage collection — which can permanently destroy deleted data before imaging. SSD forensics is significantly more complex.
  • BIOS vs. UEFI: BIOS is legacy firmware; UEFI is modern, supports GPT, Secure Boot, and larger drives. UEFI logs and Secure Boot state may be forensically relevant.
  • MBR vs. GPT: MBR (Master Boot Record) supports up to 4 primary partitions and 2TB drives; GPT (GUID Partition Table) supports 128 partitions and very large drives. Both contain partition layout information relevant to evidence location.
  • Storage interfaces: SATA, NVMe, USB — each has different acquisition considerations. NVMe drives may require specialized adapters for hardware write blocking.
  • Forensic significance: Understanding hardware helps you select the right acquisition method and anticipate evidence loss risks (e.g., SSD TRIM during power-on).

🔎 Self-Check — Week 4

Why is recovering deleted files from a solid-state drive (SSD) more challenging than from a traditional hard disk drive (HDD)?

Most Testable Concepts

  • File system types: FAT32 (no journaling, simple), NTFS (journaling, MFT, permissions, VSS), ext4 (Linux journaling), APFS (macOS, copy-on-write). Know which OS uses which.
  • How deletion works: Deleting a file typically marks the directory entry and allocation table entry as available — the data remains in unallocated space until overwritten. This is the basis for deleted file recovery.
  • File carving: Recovering files from unallocated space using file header/footer signatures (magic bytes) — does not rely on file system metadata. Used when metadata is corrupted or overwritten.
  • MFT (Master File Table): NTFS structure that stores metadata for every file and directory. Each MFT entry contains timestamps, file size, permissions, and data runs (pointers to data on disk). Critical for timeline analysis.
  • Timestamps: On NTFS — Created, Modified, Accessed, MFT-Modified ($STANDARD_INFORMATION vs. $FILE_NAME). On ext4 — mtime (modified), atime (accessed), ctime (metadata changed, not created). Know the difference.
  • Unallocated space: Disk space not currently assigned to any file. Contains remnants of deleted files. Examined using tools like Autopsy or The Sleuth Kit.

🔎 Self-Check — Week 5

A forensic examiner is attempting to recover deleted image files from a damaged NTFS volume where the Master File Table (MFT) is corrupted. Which technique is most appropriate?

Most Testable Concepts

  • Forensic imaging vs. logical copy: A forensic image is a bit-for-bit copy of the entire storage medium (including unallocated space, slack space, and deleted data). A logical copy copies only active files — insufficient for forensic purposes.
  • Write blockers: Hardware or software devices that prevent any writes to the evidence drive during acquisition. Hardware write blockers are preferred for court-admissible evidence.
  • Acquisition tools: dd (Linux command-line), dcfldd (dd with hashing), FTK Imager (GUI, supports E01 format), Guymager (Linux GUI). Know the basic purpose of each.
  • Hash verification: Compute MD5 or SHA-256 hash of the source drive and the forensic image — hashes must match to confirm integrity. SHA-256 is preferred; MD5 is considered cryptographically weak but still widely used in forensics for speed.
  • Image formats: Raw/DD (simple bit stream), E01/EWF (Expert Witness Format — includes metadata, compression, hash verification), AFF (Advanced Forensic Format).
  • Evidence preservation: Photograph evidence before collection, use anti-static bags for electronic media, maintain proper temperature/humidity, document all handling.

🔎 Self-Check — Week 6

After creating a forensic image of a suspect hard drive, an examiner computes the SHA-256 hash of the image file and finds it does NOT match the hash recorded during acquisition. What does this indicate?

Most Testable Concepts

  • Registry hives: SYSTEM, SOFTWARE, SAM, SECURITY (in C:\Windows\System32\config\), NTUSER.DAT (per-user, in user profile). Each stores different categories of configuration and artifact data.
  • SAM hive: Stores local user account information and password hashes. Critical for user account analysis and password recovery.
  • NTUSER.DAT: Per-user Registry hive. Contains RecentDocs, UserAssist (program execution with timestamps), MRU (most recently used) lists, and typed URLs.
  • UserAssist: Registry key tracking programs executed via Windows Explorer. Values are ROT-13 encoded. Provides execution timestamps and run counts.
  • RecentDocs / Jump Lists: Track recently accessed files and applications. Jump Lists are stored as LNK files and include timestamps, file paths, and volume serial numbers.
  • Shellbags: Registry keys recording folder navigation history — even for folders that no longer exist or were on removable media. Valuable for proving a user accessed specific locations.
  • RegRipper: Tool for automated extraction of forensic artifacts from Registry hives. Produces human-readable output from binary hive files.

🔎 Self-Check — Week 7

An investigator wants to determine whether a specific executable was run on a Windows system, and when. Which Windows Registry artifact is most directly useful for this purpose?

Most Testable Concepts

  • Windows Event Log format: Binary EVTX files stored in C:\Windows\System32\winevt\Logs\. Three primary logs: Security (logon events, privilege use), System (service starts/stops, hardware), Application (application errors and events).
  • Key Security Event IDs: 4624 (successful logon), 4625 (failed logon), 4648 (logon with explicit credentials), 4672 (special privileges assigned), 4688 (process creation — requires audit policy), 4698/4702 (scheduled task created/modified).
  • Prefetch files: Located in C:\Windows\Prefetch\. Store evidence of program execution — file name, run count, last run timestamps (up to 8), and files/directories accessed. Enabled by default on workstations; often disabled on servers.
  • LNK files: Windows shortcut files automatically created when a file is opened. Contain the target file path, timestamps, volume serial number, and MAC address of the system — even if the target file is deleted.
  • Volume Shadow Copies (VSS): Point-in-time snapshots of volumes. Can be used to recover earlier versions of files or Registry hives — forensically valuable and also used by ransomware attackers (who delete them).
  • NTFS artifacts: $MFT (file metadata), $LogFile (NTFS journal — recent metadata changes), $UsnJrnl (change journal — file creation, deletion, rename events with timestamps).
  • Timestomping: Anti-forensic technique where an attacker modifies file timestamps to hide activity. $STANDARD_INFORMATION timestamps can be altered; $FILE_NAME timestamps are harder to modify — discrepancies between the two indicate tampering.

Exam Strategy and Tips

📖

Reading Scenario Questions

  • Read the entire scenario before looking at answer choices.
  • Identify the role (examiner, IR analyst, legal counsel) and the goal (preserve evidence, identify attacker, prove execution).
  • Eliminate answers that would compromise evidence integrity first.
  • When two answers seem correct, choose the one that best preserves forensic soundness.
  • Watch for key words: "first," "most appropriate," "primary reason" — these change the correct answer.
✍️

Short-Answer Format

  • Answer the specific question asked — do not write everything you know about a topic.
  • Use precise forensic terminology (e.g., "chain of custody," "write blocker," "unallocated space").
  • Structure answers: state the concept, explain why it matters, give a brief example if helpful.
  • For "explain why" questions, always connect your answer to evidence integrity or legal admissibility.
  • Partial credit is typically awarded — write something for every question.
🗂️

Using Lab Reports as Study Material

  • Review your Lab 1–8 reports before the exam — the procedures you documented are the procedures being tested.
  • Pay attention to any instructor feedback on your reports — corrected errors are likely exam topics.
  • Re-read your chain of custody forms from Lab 2 — the fields and their purposes are testable.
  • Your Lab 6 imaging workflow directly maps to acquisition questions on the exam.
⏱️

Time Management During the Exam

  • Skim all questions first to gauge difficulty distribution.
  • Answer multiple-choice questions you are confident about first.
  • Flag uncertain questions and return to them — do not get stuck.
  • Allocate more time to scenario-based questions; they require careful reading.
  • Leave 5–10 minutes at the end to review flagged questions.
🔗

Connecting Concepts Across Weeks

  • Chain of custody (W2) + hash verification (W6) = evidence integrity — these topics appear together on the exam.
  • Order of volatility (W2) + dead vs. live acquisition (W6, W9) = acquisition decision framework.
  • File deletion (W5) + unallocated space recovery (W5) + file carving (W5) = one connected workflow.
  • Registry artifacts (W7) + Event Logs (W8) = Windows activity reconstruction — expect combined scenarios.
  • Linux directories (W9) + authentication logs (W9) = Linux intrusion analysis — connects to W2 investigation process.
⚠️

Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing ctime on Linux (metadata change time) with file creation time.
  • Stating that the Fourth Amendment applies to private investigators — it does not.
  • Recommending read-write mounting of forensic images — always read-only.
  • Forgetting that Prefetch is often disabled on Windows Server — it is a workstation artifact.
  • Mixing up which hive stores what: SAM = local accounts; NTUSER.DAT = per-user activity.
