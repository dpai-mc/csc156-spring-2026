  • Remembering to document every action
  • Writing objective vs. subjective observations

Option B: Investigation Phase Priorities

The six-phase investigation process (Identification, Preservation, Collection, Examination, Analysis, Presentation) must be followed in order. However, in real investigations, time and resource constraints may create pressure to cut corners.

Discuss: Which phase do you think is most critical to get right, and why? What might happen if that phase is rushed or done improperly? Use examples from the week's modules to support your reasoning.

Option C: Chain of Custody Vulnerabilities

Chain of custody documentation proves evidence integrity from collection through court presentation. However, evidence often passes through many hands during an investigation.

Analyze: What do you think is the most vulnerable point in the chain of custody? Consider evidence transfers, storage periods, multiple examiners, shipping evidence to labs, or long-term storage. What safeguards could minimize risks at this vulnerable point?

Option D: Ethics in Practice

Module 5 presented ethical scenarios involving pressure from clients, working outside your competence, and handling incidental findings.

Reflect: Which ethical principle (objectivity, competence, confidentiality, or integrity) do you think would be most difficult to maintain under real-world pressure? Why? How might you prepare yourself to uphold this principle when faced with challenging situations?

Example Initial Post (Option A: Documentation Challenges)

Posted by: Sample Student | Thursday, 8:30 PM | 312 words

I believe the most challenging aspect of maintaining proper documentation during forensic investigations is writing objective observations rather than subjective interpretations, especially under time pressure during evidence collection.

Module 5 showed the stark difference between writing "The suspect's laptop was full of stolen files" (subjective) versus "Laptop computer collected from subject's desk. Forensic analysis will determine contents" (objective). This distinction seems straightforward in examples, but I think it would be incredibly difficult in practice. When you're investigating a suspected crime, your brain naturally wants to interpret what you're seeing‚Äîespecially if the case details suggest guilt. The human tendency toward confirmation bias means we might unconsciously phrase our observations in ways that support our assumptions.

The consequences of subjective documentation are severe. As Module 4 emphasized, chain of custody and field notes must withstand legal scrutiny. If an opposing attorney can show that an investigator's notes reveal bias or predetermined conclusions, the entire investigation's credibility suffers. The BTK case from Week 1 succeeded partly because investigators documented facts objectively‚Äîthey didn't assume the floppy disk would yield evidence, they simply documented what they found (metadata showing "Dennis" and "Christ Lutheran Church").

To overcome this challenge, I think investigators need deliberate strategies: First, use the "just the facts" test‚Äîwould this observation be the same regardless of who the suspect is? Second, separate observation notes from analysis notes (Module 5's documentation types). Third, have a colleague review field notes for subjective language before finalizing them. Finally, remember that objective documentation actually strengthens your case by demonstrating professional impartiality. Defense attorneys can't claim bias if your notes are purely factual.

Documentation quality truly is "destiny" as Module 5 stated‚Äîit determines whether your technical work survives the courtroom.

Example Response to Above Post:

Great analysis of the objectivity challenge! I hadn't considered how confirmation bias might unconsciously influence our documentation language. Your point about the BTK case is particularly insightful‚Äîthe investigators could have written "suspect's disk" but instead documented it as simply "purple floppy disk," which kept their notes factual.

I'd add another strategy: the "read it to a jury" test. If you wouldn't feel comfortable reading your field note aloud to a jury because it sounds biased, rewrite it. This mental exercise might help catch subjective language before it makes it into official documentation.

Your point about separating observation notes from analysis notes is crucial. Module 5 showed that analysis happens in Phase 4 (Analysis), not during Phase 2 (Collection). Keeping these documentation types separate would help maintain objectivity during collection when bias is most likely to creep in.

Example Initial Post (Option B: Investigation Phase Priorities)

Posted by: Sample Student | Thursday, 10:45 PM | 287 words

While all six phases are important, I believe Preservation (Phase 2) is the most critical phase to get right because errors here are irreversible and contaminate everything that follows.

Module 2 emphasized that preservation must happen immediately after identification, before any examination or analysis. The reason is simple: digital evidence is fragile and volatile. If you fail to properly preserve evidence‚Äîby not using write blockers, powering on devices incorrectly, or allowing network connections‚Äîyou've potentially altered the evidence permanently. Unlike other phases where mistakes might be correctable, preservation errors can't be undone.

Consider the Target breach example from Week 1. Network forensics revealed the attack vector and data exfiltration paths. But if Target's incident response team had failed to preserve network logs properly (letting them rotate and delete, or not capturing memory dumps from infected POS systems), the forensic analysis would have been impossible. The entire investigation chain depends on having pristine evidence to examine.

Module 3 showed that even the best examination and analysis skills are worthless if you're working with contaminated evidence. An examiner might find critical evidence, but if the defense can show the evidence wasn't properly preserved, it becomes inadmissible. The IOCE Principle 1 from Module 5 states: "No action should change evidence." Preservation is where this principle is most vulnerable.

If preservation is rushed, you might: power on a device that should stay off (changing timestamps), fail to document the device state, skip photographing evidence in situ, or forget to use write blockers. Each mistake potentially destroys evidence or creates legal challenges to admissibility. That's why Module 2 stressed that preservation takes time and cannot be rushed‚Äîthe entire investigation depends on getting this phase right.
