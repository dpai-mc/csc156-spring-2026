üìä 1. The Documentation Pyramid

Why Documentation Matters

Documentation is the foundation of forensic work. Without proper documentation, your technical skills are worthless in court. Documentation proves:

  • What you did (procedures followed)
  • When you did it (timeline of activities)
  • How you did it (methodology and tools)
  • What you found (observations and results)
  • Why it matters (analysis and conclusions)

Remember: "If it's not documented, it didn't happen" in the eyes of the court.

üìê The Forensic Documentation Hierarchy

Forensic documentation follows a hierarchical structure, with each level building upon the previous:

Level 4: Formal Reports
Final deliverable for stakeholders and court
Level 3: Photographic Documentation
Visual evidence of procedures and findings
Level 2: Case Logs & Technical Worksheets
Chronological records and tool outputs
Level 1: Field Notes (Foundation)
Contemporaneous observations and actions

üîç How the Pyramid Works

Bottom-Up Construction: Each level supports and validates the levels above it.

  • Field notes are taken in real-time during the investigation
  • Case logs and worksheets are compiled from field notes and tool outputs
  • Photographs provide visual corroboration of written documentation
  • Formal reports synthesize all documentation into a coherent narrative

Legal Validation: If your report is challenged in court, you must be able to trace every statement back through the pyramid to your original field notes.

üîó How Documentation Connects to Previous Modules

Module 1: Forensic Readiness

Forensic readiness planning includes establishing documentation standards, templates, and procedures before incidents occur.

Documentation supports readiness by ensuring consistent, professional practices.

Modules 2-3: Investigation Process

Every phase of the investigation lifecycle requires specific documentation: collection logs, examination worksheets, analysis notes, and presentation reports.

Documentation runs through ALL six phases continuously.

Module 4: Chain of Custody

Chain of custody forms are one type of documentation. Field notes document evidence handling, and case logs track all custody transfers.

Documentation proves evidence integrity throughout the investigation.

üìù 2. Field Notes: The Foundation

What Are Field Notes?

Field notes are contemporaneous, handwritten records of observations, actions, and findings made during a forensic investigation. They are created at the time of observation, not reconstructed later from memory.

Key characteristics:

  • Contemporaneous: Written at the time events occur
  • Chronological: Entries in time order with timestamps
  • Objective: Facts and observations, not opinions
  • Complete: Include all relevant details
  • Legible: Readable by others
  • Permanent: Written in ink, corrections properly documented

‚úçÔ∏è What to Include in Field Notes

‚úÖ Always Include

  • Date and time of each entry
  • Location of activity
  • People present (names, roles)
  • Actions taken (what you did)
  • Observations made (what you saw/found)
  • Tools and commands used
  • Environmental conditions (if relevant)
  • Evidence collected (item numbers)
  • Problems encountered
  • Decisions made and rationale

‚ùå Never Include

  • Personal opinions about guilt/innocence
  • Speculation about motives
  • Inflammatory language
  • Irrelevant personal details
  • Assumptions not based on evidence
  • Hearsay (unless clearly marked as such)
  • Unprofessional comments
  • Information from privileged sources

‚úèÔ∏è Objective vs. Subjective Language

Field notes must use objective, factual language. Compare these examples:

‚ùå Subjective (Inappropriate)

"The suspect's laptop was full of stolen files. He's obviously guilty of data theft. The laptop looked suspicious and was probably used for illegal activities."

Problems:

  • Assumes guilt ("obviously guilty")
  • Uses inflammatory language ("stolen")
  • Makes conclusions without evidence
  • Includes speculation ("probably used")

‚úÖ Objective (Appropriate)

"Laptop computer (HP EliteBook 840 G8, SN: 5CG04217XM) collected from subject's desk. Device was powered on, displaying Windows 11 login screen. Forensic analysis will determine contents and user activity."

Strengths:

  • States facts only
  • Neutral language
  • Specific device identification
  • Defers conclusions to analysis phase

üîß Making Corrections in Field Notes

‚ö†Ô∏è NEVER Erase, White-Out, or Obliterate Errors

Corrections must be visible and documented. Any attempt to hide mistakes will be viewed as evidence tampering.

Proper correction method:

  1. Draw a single line through the error (text remains readable)
  2. Write the correct information nearby
  3. Initial and date the correction
  4. Optionally, briefly note reason for correction
EXAMPLE: Proper Correction Method 
10:35 - Device serial number: 5CG04217XN 5CG04217XM [Initials: JD, 10:36]
        (Correction: Misread final digit, verified against device label)

üìÅ 3. Types of Forensic Documentation

Beyond field notes, forensic investigations require multiple types of documentation, each serving a specific purpose.

Case logs are chronological records of all activities related to a case, compiled from field notes and other sources.

Purpose:

  • Track all evidence items
  • Document all personnel involved
  • Record all analysis activities

üì∑ Photographic Documentation

Photographic documentation provides visual evidence of scenes, devices, procedures, and findings.

Purpose:

  • Preserve visual record of evidence location and condition
  • Document physical characteristics of devices
  • Show context and relationships
  • Corroborate written documentation
  • Communicate findings to non-technical audiences

üì∏ Photographic Documentation Best Practices

Required Photo Sequence:

  1. Overall/Establishing shot: Wide view showing entire scene/context
  2. Mid-range shot: Shows evidence and immediate surroundings
  3. Close-up: Details of evidence (serial numbers, labels, damage)
  4. With scale: Include ruler or scale reference

Photo Log Requirements:

  • Photo number (sequential)
  • Date and time taken
  • Photographer name
  • Camera/device used
  • Description of what's shown
  • Relationship to evidence items

üìÑ Formal Reports

Formal reports are the final deliverable that synthesizes all documentation into a coherent narrative for stakeholders and courts.

Standard Report Sections:

  1. Executive Summary: High-level overview for non-technical readers
  2. Case Information: Case number, dates, requesting party, examiner
  3. Evidence Description: Complete description of all evidence examined
  4. Examination Objectives: What questions the analysis sought to answer
  5. Methodology: Tools, procedures, and standards followed
  6. Findings: Detailed results of examination and analysis
  7. Conclusions: Interpretation of findings relative to objectives
  8. Appendices: Technical details, tool outputs, photo logs

‚öñÔ∏è 4. Professional Standards & Guidelines

Forensic examiners must follow established professional standards to ensure quality, consistency, and legal defensibility.

üèõÔ∏è Key Standards Organizations

SWGDE

Scientific Working Group on Digital Evidence

Develops best practice guidelines for digital evidence collection, examination, and reporting. SWGDE documents are widely accepted in U.S. courts.

Key Documents:

  • Best Practices for Computer Forensics
  • Digital & Multimedia Evidence Glossary
  • Model Quality Assurance Manual

NIST

National Institute of Standards and Technology

Federal agency that develops standards and guidelines for digital forensics, including tool testing and validation.

Key Resources:

  • Computer Forensics Tool Testing (CFTT)
  • Guide to Integrating Forensic Techniques
  • Mobile Device Forensics Guidelines

IOCE

International Organization on Computer Evidence

International forum for law enforcement agencies to exchange information on computer evidence examination.

Core Principles:

  • Evidence integrity preservation
  • Documented methodology
  • Competent personnel
  • Appropriate tools and techniques

üìè IOCE Six Principles for Digital Evidence

The IOCE established six fundamental principles that guide all digital forensic work:

  1. Principle 1: No Action Should Change Evidence
    Upon seizing digital evidence, actions taken should not change that evidence. If change is unavoidable, document the change and its impact.
  2. Principle 2: Competent Personnel Only
    When it is necessary to access original digital evidence, the person doing so must be trained and competent for the task.
  3. Principle 3: Document All Activity
    An audit trail or other record of all processes applied to digital evidence should be created and preserved. An independent third party should be able to examine those processes and achieve the same result.
  4. Principle 4: Individual Responsibility
    The individual is responsible for all actions taken with respect to digital evidence while it is in their possession.
  5. Principle 5: Agency Responsibility
    The agency that seizes, accesses, stores, or transfers digital evidence is responsible for compliance with these principles.
  6. Principle 6: Legal Compliance
    All activities conducted must comply with applicable laws, regulations, and policies.

ü§ù 5. Professional Ethics in Forensic Practice

Why Ethics Matter in Digital Forensics

Digital forensic examiners have extraordinary access to private information and significant power to impact lives through their findings. Ethical conduct is not optional‚Äîit's fundamental to the profession.

Ethical violations can result in:

  • Evidence exclusion from court
  • Case dismissal
  • Loss of professional certifications
  • Civil liability
  • Criminal prosecution
  • Damage to employer's reputation

‚öñÔ∏è Core Ethical Principles

üéØ Objectivity

Principle: Remain impartial and unbiased. Let the evidence speak for itself.

In Practice:

  • Report all findings, even if they contradict expectations
  • Don't let personal opinions influence analysis
  • Avoid confirmation bias
  • Present limitations of findings honestly

üí° Competence

Principle: Work only within your area of expertise and training.

In Practice:

  • Acknowledge limitations of your knowledge
  • Seek expert consultation when needed
  • Maintain current skills through training
  • Don't overstate qualifications

üîí Confidentiality

Principle: Protect sensitive information and privacy.

In Practice:

  • Secure evidence and documentation
  • Limit access to authorized personnel
  • Don't discuss cases publicly
  • Respect attorney-client privilege

‚úÖ Integrity

Principle: Be honest and transparent in all professional activities.

In Practice:

  • Never fabricate or alter evidence
  • Admit mistakes promptly
  • Document procedures truthfully
  • Resist pressure to reach predetermined conclusions

  • Complete - include all relevant details
  • Legible - readable by others
  • Permanent - written in ink, corrections properly documented

Professional Standards:

  • SWGDE provides best practice guidelines for digital evidence
  • NIST develops standards and validates forensic tools
  • IOCE Six Principles guide all digital forensic work

  • Competence - work within your expertise
  • Confidentiality - protect sensitive information
  • Integrity - be honest and transparent
